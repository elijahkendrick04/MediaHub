# Organic-First Campaign Plan — DebugForge (placeholder name)

Brief confirms all 8 fields; this is Mode A — proceeding directly without interview. Understanding: win 50 paying B2B SaaS customers in 6 months among backend engineers at Series A–B startups via a founder-led organic campaign against a category incumbent that outspends on paid by ~400:1.

## 1. Campaign Ideas

### Concept A — "We Quit Sentry" (Counter-Narrative)
**Thesis:** Backend engineers at Series A–B startups are quietly resentful of paying $400/seat/month for a tool that feels enterprise-bloated. Naming that resentment publicly — and owning the "we switched" category — is a positioning move the incumbent cannot respond to without looking defensive.
**Archetype:** Counter-narrative + Founder-Story Arc
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (founder LinkedIn/Twitter) + Tier 2 (earned media via dev newsletters)
**Authenticity hook:** The founder's own true migration story — the actual cost line, the actual bloat complaint, the before/after. This is not a marketing claim; it is a sourced decision with receipts.
**Minimum viable version:** One 800-word founder Twitter/X thread: "We built an error monitor because we couldn't justify $400/seat anymore. Here is what we found." UTM-tagged link to trial. Measure thread saves and trial signups in 48h.

---

### Concept B — "Changelog as Campaign" (Founder-Story Arc / Product-Led)
**Thesis:** Developer tools earn trust through the product itself. Weekly unpolished changelogs posted by the founder on LinkedIn and X — raw, honest, "here's what we shipped and why" — compound trust faster than any ad because they signal that a real engineer is building this in public.
**Archetype:** Founder-Story Arc + Product-Led Growth
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (founder personal LinkedIn/X) + Tier 2 (Hacker News Show HN, dev newsletters)
**Authenticity hook:** The actual GitHub commits, actual customer bug report quotes, actual time spent — no sanitized "product updates," only the build diary.
**Minimum viable version:** Four consecutive weekly changelog posts on LinkedIn + X (one month). Track saves, profile visits, and trial signups per post.

---

### Concept C — "Developer Horror Stories" (Community-Build + Earned Media Stunt)
**Thesis:** Every backend engineer has an "outage war story" tied to bad error monitoring. Make the product the solution to a genre of real, felt pain that engineers already talk about in private. Crowdsource the stories, build a free public archive, get engineers to submit and share.
**Archetype:** Community-Build + Earned-Media Stunt
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (community node — GitHub Discussions or public Notion page) + Tier 2 (X/LinkedIn amplification, submission to dev newsletters)
**Authenticity hook:** The stories are real and submitted by real engineers. The product exists. The archive can be cited by journalists and newsletters as a resource, not an ad.
**Minimum viable version:** A simple public form ("Submit your error monitoring horror story") + 10 curated stories posted on the blog. Pitch to one dev newsletter. Measure referral traffic and trial starts.

---

### Concept D — "Benchmark Transparency" (Counter-Narrative + Demand Capture)
**Thesis:** The incumbent can afford conferences and SDRs but cannot publish honest pricing-to-value benchmarks without damaging their own position. The challenger can. A detailed, maintained public benchmark — "what $3k/month of error monitoring actually gets you across 5 tools" — captures high-intent search traffic that competitors will never bid against themselves.
**Archetype:** Counter-Narrative + Earned Media
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 2 (SEO, demand-capture long-tail search) + Tier 1 (founder distribution on X/LinkedIn)
**Authenticity hook:** Real numbers, sourced from real pricing pages and real usage. Methodology public. The post invites challengers. Incumbents rarely respond — their silence is itself a signal.
**Minimum viable version:** One 2,000-word comparison post — "[Incumbent] vs. [your tool] vs. [two others] at Series A scale" — published on the blog. Submitted to two dev newsletters on publication day. UTM-tagged trial link throughout. Track 90-day organic search pickup.

---

### Concept E — "First 10 Design Partners" (Referral Flywheel + Community-Build)
**Thesis:** The bottleneck is trials, not brand awareness. Instead of distributing to find trial users, recruit 10 engineers personally and in public — turn the first customer acquisition into content. The act of recruiting, onboarding, and shipping fixes to design partners is itself the campaign.
**Archetype:** Referral Flywheel + Community-Build
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (personal outreach, founder network, community nodes)
**Authenticity hook:** The design partner relationships are real, the bug fixes are real, the testimonials are unscripted. The founder posts the actual Slack message a design partner sent — with permission.
**Minimum viable version:** Send 30 personal cold LinkedIn DMs from the founder to backend engineers at target companies. Book 5 design-partner calls. Convert 3. Post the onboarding story publicly. Track downstream referrals from those 3.

## 3. Spend Asymmetry Verdict

The competitor (incumbent) spends approximately $40M/year ($3.3M/month) on marketing. The user's paid budget is $3k/month. Ratio: ~1,100:1 on paid spend.

However, the smaller competitors in your price tier (3 of them) represent a mild-asymmetry competitive environment where selective paid amplification of proven organic winners is viable and worth testing. The strategy below distinguishes between these two threat classes.

**Preconditions Check (0–6):**

2. **Accumulated grievance / unmet demand:** Backend engineers paying $400+/seat have documented resentment about incumbent pricing and feature bloat. The pain is real and widely expressed in dev communities. **Score: 1**

**Total: 5/6.** Run the organic-first playbook at full scale. ## 7. Three Alternative Campaign Shapes

### Shape A — Founder-Narrative-First ("The Engineer Who Quit the Expensive Tool")

**How it runs:** The founder spends months 1–2 building their public presence as the "I built this to escape $400/seat" voice on LinkedIn and X. Every post is a chapter in the story: the frustration, the build, the specific technical decisions, the early customers. The blog and newsletter compound the content into longer form. Trial signups flow from the founder's audience and from earned media that covers the story.

**Self/Us/Now flagship piece:** *"I was the lead backend engineer at [company]. We were paying $400 per developer per month for error monitoring. I asked what we were actually getting for that — and I couldn't answer my own CFO's question. So I built something. Here's what I learned. If you're a backend team of 15–50 and you've ever flinched at your monitoring bill, the trial link is in my bio."* (LinkedIn post + X thread, week 2)

**Tradeoffs:**
- Highest ceiling if the founder is a compelling, consistent writer and speaker. Compounds beyond the 6-month window.
- Lowest ceiling if the founder burns out at week 6 or posts inconsistently. Inconsistency kills parasocial trust faster than never starting.
- Slowest to generate trials in weeks 1–2 (trust must accumulate before it converts). Right call given the 18% trial-to-customer rate — if trials convert well, the job is getting more of them into the funnel, and founder presence is the right mechanism.

---

### Shape B — Search-Capture-First ("Own the Comparison Queries")

**How it runs:** Months 1–2 are spent publishing 4–6 deeply specific comparison posts targeting long-tail, high-intent queries that engineers type when they are actively evaluating options: "error monitoring for Node.js Series A startups," "[incumbent] pricing for 20-engineer team," "[incumbent] alternative under $1000/month." The SEO work compounds over 6 months. Paid Google Ads ($2k/month) capture the intent traffic immediately while the organic ranking builds.

**Self/Us/Now flagship piece:** *"I spent three weeks pricing out every error monitoring tool for a 20-engineer backend team. Here are the real numbers — including the ones the vendors don't publish on their pricing pages."* (2,500-word blog post; author byline is the founder, not the company)

**Tradeoffs:**
- Fastest to produce measurable trials from paid channels (week 2–3) — gives the campaign a quick signal before the organic compound starts.
- Lowest trust-building effect if it is the only shape running; comparison content without founder presence reads as SEO factory content, not a real company.
- Best choice if the founder cannot commit to consistent personal posting (shape A dependency). Can run with marketer as primary driver.

---

### Shape C — Design-Partner-First ("50 Real Engineers Before We Advertise")

**How it runs:** Month 1 is entirely direct outreach — the founder personally DMs or emails 50 backend engineers from their existing Twitter following and LinkedIn connections, not a cold audience. Target: 10 design-partner relationships with engineers who get white-glove onboarding, direct founder access, and in return provide honest feedback and — where willing — named testimonials or referrals. The campaign is built on top of this real base, not before it.

**Self/Us/Now flagship piece:** *"We onboarded 10 teams in January without running a single ad. Here is what they told us about error monitoring that we didn't expect — and what we changed because of it. If you are one of the 20 teams on our waitlist, your turn is this week."* (LinkedIn post, month 2)

**Tradeoffs:**
- Highest quality of trial and lowest cost-per-customer in the first 90 days if the founder's existing network is warm enough to convert.
- Lowest scalability past the first 50 customers without adding a channel — cannot reach 50 customers purely through personal outreach in 6 months unless the referral flywheel activates by month 3.
- Best choice if the 18% trial-to-customer rate is itself fragile and needs fixing before scaling trials. Design-partner feedback surfaces those gaps before a broad campaign exposes them.

---

**Recommended execution shape: Shape A (spine) + Shape B (SEO and paid layer).**
Shape A builds the trust and brand moat. Shape B captures engineers already evaluating. Shape C's direct-outreach discipline is incorporated into weeks 1–2 of the action list as the first-trial-generation mechanism while Shape A accumulates.

## 9. Ad Copy + Boost Rules

**Paid applies at mild asymmetry (vs. smaller competitors) with a categorical-override on the incumbent axis.**

### Creative direction

All paid creative must be indistinguishable in voice from the founder's organic posts. The rule: if you could not tell whether it was an ad or a post by reading it, you are doing it right.

**Do not:** stock illustrations, "Book a Demo" CTAs, enterprise ROI language, before/after feature comparison tables.
**Do:** founder photo (real, not headshot studio), sentence-fragment copy, specific number ("saves teams like ours $240k/year"), genuine question as headline.

**Sample LinkedIn boost copy (adapted from the flagship thread):**

> "I asked 5 backend engineers why they hadn't switched monitoring tools yet. Every single one said the same thing.
>
> [First thing they said.]
>
> We built our answer into the product. If you're running a 15–50 engineer backend on [framework], the trial is free for 60 days.
>
> [Link]"

This copy should only go live if the organic version of this post cleared ≥50 saves in the first 48 hours. If it did not, the copy has not been validated and should not be boosted.

**Sample Google Ad copy (search, non-brand):**

*Headline 1:* Error Monitoring Without the $400/Seat Bill
*Headline 2:* Built for 10–50 Engineer Teams
*Description:* No enterprise features you'll never use. Real pricing, real engineers. Free 60-day trial — no credit card.

### Boost rules

1. **24–48h organic traction gate — mandatory.** Do not boost any post until it has demonstrated: saves (not just likes), shares to non-followers, or comment thread depth >3 replies from people the founder did not tag. Likes do not qualify; they are cheap and bot-inflated.
2. **Audience definition — warm first:** LinkedIn boost → "people who visited the trial page in the last 30 days" OR "1% lookalike of trial signups." Cold audiences on LinkedIn are not in the plan until month 3 with a lift-test result in hand.
3. **Google Ads — non-brand long-tail only.** No bidding on the incumbent's brand name, no bidding on your own brand name. The incumbent dominates brand-keyword bidding at $3.3M/month; you will lose the auction and pay for traffic that either would have found you organically or never converts. Long-tail high-intent queries ("error monitoring for [framework] [team size]") have lower CPCs and higher purchase intent.
4. **Frequency cap — 5 impressions per user per 7 days.** Beyond 5 impressions, conversion rates decline and brand sentiment goes negative. Creative refresh cycle: every 14 days. If click-through rate falls >30% from initial, the creative is fatigued — retire and replace, do not increase bid.
5. **No brand-keyword bidding on your own name** without first running a geo-holdout test (Template 4 from `lift-test-templates.md`). Blake, Nosko & Tadelis showed brand-keyword spend at eBay produced near-zero incremental lift — the traffic was going to arrive organically anyway. At $3k/month total paid budget, brand-keyword cannibalism is particularly damaging.

## 10. Lift-Test / Measurement Plan

**Template 5 — Organic-source attribution (zero-budget baseline, graduating to Template 1 by month 3)**

### Month 1–2 (zero-budget baseline — Template 5)

**Hypothesis:** LinkedIn organic content converts trial signups at a higher rate per unique visitor than X/Twitter organic, and both outperform direct traffic from blog SEO. Paid Google Ads (non-brand long-tail) produce incremental trial signups on top of organic baseline.

**Implementation:**
- UTM scheme: `utm_source=linkedin|twitter|newsletter|blog|google-ads|referral`, `utm_medium=organic|paid|email`, `utm_campaign=we-quit-sentry`
- Conversion event: trial signup on the trial page (tracked in Plausible or PostHog)
- Measurement: per-channel conversion rate = trial signups / unique visitors, measured weekly
- Duration: 30 days minimum (weeks 1–4 of this plan)

**Decision rule:**
- Top channel by conversion rate → double weekly effort in month 2
- Any channel at <20% of top channel's rate → drop or reassign unless it serves a secondary function (credibility, SEO backlink)
- Overall conversion rate across all channels <0.5% → the bottleneck is upstream; re-run MMF gate before re-investing in distribution

**Caveat (stated out loud):** This is directional, not causal. Multi-touch attribution is underspecified — someone who signs up via the newsletter link may have first heard about the product on LinkedIn. Treat the rankings as effort-allocation guidance, not proof of lift.

### Month 3 (if Google Ads spend is producing signals — graduate to Template 1 / geo-holdout lite)

**Hypothesis:** Google Ads (non-brand long-tail, $2k/month) produce incremental trial signups in the treated cities above the organic-only baseline.

**Design:**
- Select 2 comparable US metros by startup density and backend-engineer population (e.g., Austin and Denver, or Seattle and Portland)
- Run Google Ads geo-targeted to one metro for 4 weeks; hold out the other
- Track trial signups (total, not platform-reported clicks) in both metros before, during, and after
- Compute difference-in-differences: (test metro post − test metro pre) − (holdout metro post − holdout metro pre)

**Minimum sample:** ~200 trial page visits per metro per 4-week window for a detectable signal at this trial size.

**Decision threshold:**
- Incremental trials ≥ 2× the cost of the Google Ads spend in that metro → continue and scale
- Incremental trials positive but < spend cost → rework keyword targeting or ad copy; do not scale
- Incremental trials statistically indistinguishable from zero → stop Google Ads, reallocate $2k to content production (marketer hours)

## 11. Anti-Vanity Metric Dashboard

### Track these

| Metric | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Trial signups per UTM source (weekly) | The only channel signal that matters — where are paying customers coming from? |
| Trial-to-paying-customer conversion rate | The brief says 18% — maintain or improve; a drop means something changed in trial experience |
| LinkedIn post saves and shares (48h window) | Real engagement signal; saves = bookmarked value, shares = advocacy. Not likes. |
| Newsletter open rate and reply rate | Open rate > 35% means the list is warm. Replies mean the content is generating conversation. |
| Founder LinkedIn follower growth from 3k (monthly) | Compound audience growth as a proxy for organic distribution reach |
| Design-partner referrals (month 2+) | How many new trials came from a design partner recommending the tool to a colleague? |
| Incremental trial lift from geo-holdout (month 3) | The only honest measurement of whether Google Ads are producing incremental value |
| Earned media placements (named target, confirmed feature) | Real third-party credibility events — not press release pickups |

### Explicitly ignore these

| Metric | Why it is a trap |
|---|---|
| Platform-reported ROAS / attributed conversions | Attributes to last-touch ad view regardless of whether the ad caused the conversion. Lewis & Rao (QJE 2015): most digital ad ROI confidence intervals span zero. |
| LinkedIn / X impressions and reach | Reach ≠ trust. The bottleneck is trust, not reach. A post with 10k impressions and 3 saves is worse than a post with 500 impressions and 40 saves. |
| Raw follower count | A 10k follower account that converts no trials is worth less than a 1k follower account whose followers are backend engineers who have seen the product and told a colleague. |
| Blog pageviews without UTM conversion data | Traffic without trial attribution is vanity. The blog exists to generate trials, not to demonstrate that people read the blog. |
| MQL count / form fills | The B2B SaaS sector failure mode per the rider: MQL theatre. Track demo-to-close and activation rate instead. |
| Email list size without open rate segmentation | A 5,000-person list with a 10% open rate is worth less than a 500-person list with a 45% open rate in a developer audience. Segment ruthlessly. |
| Google Ads click-through rate | CTR on ads tells you the creative is attracting attention; it says nothing about whether those clicks produce trials. Track trial signups per paid click, not CTR. |
| Social media engagement rate (aggregate) | Platform-reported engagement rate includes likes, which are cheap, bot-inflatable, and do not predict purchase. Use saves and shares only. |

**B2B SaaS sector failure-mode standing warning (from sector rider):** The single most common way this campaign fails is MQL theatre — optimizing for form fills and platform-attributed ROAS while actual pipeline stays flat. The dashboard above is designed to prevent this. If you find yourself in a weekly review celebrating a "great ROAS week" without a matching increase in trial signups from incremental sources, something has gone wrong and the lift-test plan in Section 10 should be run immediately.

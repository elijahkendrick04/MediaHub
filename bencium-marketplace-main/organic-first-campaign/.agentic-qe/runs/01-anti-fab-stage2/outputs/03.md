## 1. Campaign Ideas (5 concepts across archetypes)

### Concept A — "The Pricing Page That Lies"

**Archetype:** Counter-narrative

**One-line thesis:** Backend engineers who resent the $400/seat/month billing model already have the grievance — this campaign names it in their exact language and positions the tool as the honest alternative.

**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (founder LinkedIn + Twitter) + Tier 2 (earned media, developer newsletters)

**Authenticity hook:** The founder has personally paid the enterprise billing tax at a previous job — a specific invoice number, a specific conversation with finance, a specific moment where the price felt like a betrayal of the product's original promise. That real story is the campaign's spine. It cannot be manufactured.

**Minimum viable version:** One long-form LinkedIn post from the founder: "I paid [$X] per seat for [category of tool] at my last company. Here's the line item on the invoice, here's what we actually used, and here's why I built something different." Measure: saves, shares, inbound DM volume within 72 hours. If it clears 50 saves, this is the concept.

---

### Concept B — "Open Changelog"

**Archetype:** Founder-story arc / earned-media through transparency

**One-line thesis:** In a category where incumbents ship changelog entries every few months in polished release notes, a weekly raw-footage product changelog — unedited, from the founder's desk — signals that a real engineer is building this and is accountable to users in a way the incumbent is not.

**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (founder Twitter/X + LinkedIn) + Tier 2 (distribution through developer newsletters and subreddits)

**Authenticity hook:** Raw terminal output, actual error traces, real customer names (with permission) or anonymized but specific use cases appearing in each episode. The "shows its work" format is the differentiator. Agency-produced cannot replicate it; a competitor responding with a better-produced video loses.

**Minimum viable version:** Three consecutive weekly changelog posts in text format — no video required — on Twitter/X. Each post: one specific bug fixed, one specific customer who reported it, one specific thing that changed in the codebase. Measure: replies from engineers asking to try the product within 2 weeks.

---

### Concept C — "The Better-Error Slack Challenge"

**Archetype:** Community-build / product-led growth

**One-line thesis:** Backend engineers are already sharing error screenshots in Slack internally; the campaign turns that behavior into a public artifact — engineers post their worst, most cryptic error experience, the tool shows what a better error message looks like, and the format spreads peer-to-peer across engineering Slack channels.

**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (community node + word-of-mouth referral) + Tier 2 (organic Twitter/X amplification)

**Authenticity hook:** Real error traces from real production systems. The "before/after" format is instantly legible to any backend engineer and viscerally confirms the problem. No creative brief, no agency, no copywriter required — the engineers submit the content by entering the product.

**Minimum viable version:** Seed with 10 real errors from the founder's own experience or from 3 early customers (with permission). Post the before/after thread on Twitter/X. Add a "submit yours" CTA. Measure: submissions from engineers who did not already know about the product within 2 weeks.

---

### Concept D — "The 5-Engineer Trust Ladder"

**Archetype:** Referral flywheel

**One-line thesis:** Series A–B startups at 10–50 engineers have a tight internal trust network; one engineer champion who moves from trial to paying customer and tells their Slack channel is worth more than any ad — the campaign systematizes this peer path with a low-friction referral mechanic that rewards the engineer who refers, not the company.

**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (word-of-mouth referral + founder direct outreach)

**Authenticity hook:** The referral reward is not a discount or a gift card — it is a specific developer-facing perk (early feature access, a private Slack channel with the founder for roadmap input, a named "founding customer" status). The reward must be something engineers actually want, confirmed by asking 5 trial users before launching.

**Minimum viable version:** Personally email the 18 trial-to-paid converts you already have (18% of however many trials). Ask one question: "Is there one other team in your network dealing with the same [error monitoring] problem?" No referral link required in week 1 — just the ask. Measure: warm introductions made within 14 days.

---

### Concept E — "The Error Rate Post-Mortem Series"

**Archetype:** Earned-media stunt / coalition play with developer content creators

**One-line thesis:** Engineers at Series A–B startups have lived through production incidents — the campaign produces a structured "public post-mortem" format that names the error, the monitoring gap that hid it, and the fix, and invites engineering leads to co-publish with the founder as named contributors.

**Primary channel tier:** Tier 2 (earned media via developer newsletters, podcast appearances, HackerNews) + Tier 1 (founder LinkedIn/Twitter amplification)

**Authenticity hook:** Each post-mortem involves a real engineer from a real startup describing a real incident. The named contributor (with their permission) is the credibility signal. The tool appears in the "how we found it" section as a specific artifact, not a sponsored mention.

**Minimum viable version:** One post-mortem written jointly with one early customer, published on the company blog and cross-posted to HackerNews with the customer as co-author. Measure: HN upvotes within 48 hours and inbound trial requests in the 7 days following.

---

**Select one or more concepts to push through Stages 3–5.** Each has a different risk profile and time-to-traction curve. Concept A and B are the fastest to execute. Concept C requires early customer cooperation. Concept D is the highest-leverage if the existing 18% trial conversion rate is real. Concept E takes longest but builds the most durable earned-media asset.

**For this plan, Concept A (Counter-narrative) is selected as the primary concept, with Concept D (Referral flywheel) as the simultaneous lower-effort track.** Both are consistent with the brief's bottleneck (trust, not reach), the capacity constraint (21h/week total), and the 6-month time window. The reasoning: the counter-narrative provides top-of-funnel pull; the referral flywheel converts the existing trial base without additional top-of-funnel spend. They do not compete for the same effort.

## 3. Spend Asymmetry Verdict

**Your asymmetry is categorical on paid spend (competitor spends ~13,000× your $3k/month paid budget on an annual basis).** However, this is not the whole picture: the brief describes the bottleneck as trust, not reach, and the relevant ratio is not just spend — it is effectiveness per dollar spent on the target audience. The $400/seat incumbent is spending $40M/year to reach a much wider market than your specific target (backend engineers at 10–50 person Series A–B startups). Your narrow-audience specificity means you can dominate a sub-segment the incumbent's broad campaigns are too blunt to address.

The practical implication: **you are categorical on paid, but competitive on organic narrative** if you speak to the specific pain of the specific buyer segment precisely enough that the incumbent's generic developer marketing cannot answer it. This is the leverage point. The plan leads with organic Tier 1–2, uses paid only as surgical amplification of organic winners, and never treats $3k/month paid as a demand-generation mechanism.

### Preconditions Check (scored against the 6 factors from the Hungarian case study)

1. **Credible insider / founder story.** The founder is a developer who has personally experienced the problem they are solving. The paid enterprise billing tax is a specific, lived experience. Score: **present.**

2. **Accumulated grievance.** Engineers at startups have been paying $400/seat/month for a tool whose pricing model feels extractive — the grievance is not hypothetical, it is a real category frustration visible in developer forums and Slack channels. The incumbent's pricing model has been criticized publicly. Score: **present.**

3. **Consolidated challenger field.** There are 3 smaller competitors in the same price tier. This is a partially fragmented field — the campaign must position as the clear alternative to the incumbent specifically, not as one of four challengers. If buyers perceive this tool as similar to the 3 price-tier competitors, the organic narrative dilutes. This is a sub-goal: the counter-narrative must separate this product from the 3 smaller competitors, not just from the incumbent. Score: **partial (flag as sub-goal).**

4. **Felt pain, not abstract pain.** Every backend engineer at a Series A–B startup has either paid this category's pricing directly or watched their team absorb it. The pain is felt, not distributed or abstract. Score: **present.**

5. **Threshold-rewarding system.** B2B SaaS does not have an electoral threshold, but it has a network-effect analog: when 3–4 engineers at a company use a tool and it becomes the default, switching costs create retention. The "champion engineer → seat expansion" dynamic rewards the first tool to win a team's trust. Score: **partially present — the threshold dynamic is real but slower than electoral math.**

6. **Incumbent overplaying a fear / saturation hand.** The $40M/year incumbent is running broad developer marketing (conferences, paid search, SDR outbound) in a market where developers have learned to ignore vendor marketing. The saturation of generic developer ads is the opening for an organic, developer-first counter-narrative. Score: **present.**

**Score: 5/6 preconditions.** The one gap — consolidated challenger field — becomes a campaign sub-goal: the positioning must explicitly separate this product from the 3 price-tier competitors, not just from the incumbent. The plan flags this at the positioning stage (Section 6).

**Verdict: run the organic-first playbook at full scale. Proceed to Stage 3a.**

## 4. Channel Tier Stack

**Primary function: Trust compounding.** The brief names the bottleneck explicitly: developers trust other developers, not vendors. Cold paid ads do not work for this buyer. The function is not demand capture (demand exists — developers are already looking for alternatives) and not paid amplification (organic winners do not yet exist at scale). The function is building the founder-developer credibility that compresses the trust gap from "I've heard of them" to "my colleague uses this."

**B2B SaaS sector rider (Rider 2) applied:**

- Primary channel: Tier 1 (founder LinkedIn + Twitter/X) + Tier 2 (SEO on high-intent non-brand queries, developer newsletters, podcast guesting on engineering shows)
- Archetype defaults: founder-story arc, earned media through in-depth technical content, demand capture for existing search volume
- Failure-mode warning: MQL theatre — do not let the marketer's 15h/week default to form-fill optimization, webinar production, or LinkedIn carousel campaigns that generate activity without pipeline. Every week, the question is: did this produce a trial or a conversation with a qualified prospect?

**Conflict flag:** The B2B SaaS rider biases toward founder-LinkedIn + demand capture. The function-first choice (trust compounding) biases toward Tier 1 organic. These are consistent — founder LinkedIn *is* trust compounding for B2B SaaS. No conflict.

**Channel Stack — Prioritized:**

| Priority | Channel | Tier | Function | Weekly Effort |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Founder LinkedIn (long-form posts, specific technical/pricing stories) | 1 | Trust compounding | 2h/week (founder) |
| 2 | Founder Twitter/X (shorter versions, thread format, community engagement) | 1 | Trust compounding | 1.5h/week (founder) |
| 3 | SEO content — high-intent long-tail queries ("error monitoring for small teams," "alternative to $400/seat [category]," "[category] pricing comparison") | 2 | Demand capture | 4h/week (marketer) |
| 4 | Developer newsletter outreach (earned media — targeting existing newsletters read by backend engineers at startups) | 2 | Trust compounding via borrowed credibility | 3h/week (marketer) |
| 5 | Podcast guest appearances — engineering/startup operator shows | 2 | Trust compounding via earned media | 2h/week (marketer research + scheduling; founder appears) |
| 6 | Referral flywheel — direct outreach to existing trial converters | 1 | Word-of-mouth / trust transfer | 1.5h/week (founder) |
| 7 | Warm retargeting — website visitors, blog readers (paid, Tier 3) | 3 | Paid amplification of warm audience | 1h/week (marketer setup + monitoring) |
| 8 | Long-tail non-brand Google Search ads (paid, Tier 3) | 3 | Demand capture — high-intent search terms only | 2h/week (marketer) |

**Channels explicitly excluded:**

- Broad cold Meta / LinkedIn ads (Tier 4): the buyer explicitly does not respond to cold vendor ads. The brief confirms it. Do not spend $3k/month here.
- Brand-keyword Google bidding: run a brand-keyword holdout test (Template 4 in Section 10) before spending on brand terms. The eBay research strongly suggests this is cannibalized free traffic.
- Generic display advertising: Tier 4, near-zero ROI for developer tools.
- TikTok: not the platform for backend engineers at Series A–B startups in the 2026 landscape for this category. May revisit if content experiments show otherwise.

**Total weekly effort: 17h/week** (Founder: 5h; Marketer: 12h). This is within the 21h/week total capacity. The 4h buffer is held for async coordination, brief reviews, and campaign monitoring.

## 5. 70/30 Allocation

**Allocation: 70% organic / 30% paid.** Mild-asymmetry rules apply within the paid budget constraint.

| | Effort allocation | Budget allocation |
|---|---|---|
| Organic (Tier 1–2) | ~70% of 21h/week = ~14.7h/week | $5k/month ($3k retained for paid) |
| Paid (Tier 3 only) | ~30% of 21h/week = ~6.3h/week setup + monitoring | $3k/month |

**Rationale:**

The paid asymmetry is categorical (1:13,000) but the bottleneck is trust, not reach, which means the 70/30 split is the right structural choice — not because we can outspend the incumbent (we cannot), but because paid at $3k/month is most effective as a surgical amplifier of proven organic content, not as a demand-generation mechanism. The $3k/month paid budget is allocated entirely to Tier 3: warm retargeting and long-tail search, never to cold audiences.

Within organic, Tier 1 (founder personal channels) takes priority over Tier 2 (SEO, earned media). The bottleneck is trust, not reach, and the trust-compounding function is served by the founder's voice, not by search rankings or newsletter mentions alone.

**Paid budget breakdown ($3k/month):**

- $1,500/month: Long-tail non-brand Google Search ads on high-intent queries. This is demand capture — people already searching for the category, not brand keywords.
- $1,000/month: Warm retargeting (website visitors who did not trial, blog readers). Audience must come from organic inbound; no cold audience.
- $500/month: Boosting top-performing organic posts (LinkedIn, Twitter/X) — only after 24–48h organic traction gate is cleared (saves, shares, deep comments, not raw likes).

## 6. Competitor Saturation Map

I am describing competitor patterns at the category level because specific incumbent and competitor names were not provided in the brief. Name them and I will sharpen this section immediately.

### The $400-per-seat incumbent

**(a) What they saturate:**

- Conference sponsorships and booth presence at major engineering and DevOps conferences — high-visibility, high-production, high-cost brand presence that engineers walk past.
- Paid search on broad developer-tools queries — generic category terms, their own brand keywords, and competitor comparison keywords.
- SDR outbound sequences targeting engineering managers and CTOs at mid-size and enterprise companies — volume-based, personalization-light, vendor-speak heavy.
- Blog content that reads as marketing — feature announcements, case studies with enterprise logos, "best practices" guides optimized for search volume rather than developer trust.
- LinkedIn sponsored posts with polished carousel graphics, stock illustrations, and growth-hack CTAs ("See how [Logo] reduced error response time by 47%").

**(b) The absence that becomes your signal:**

The incumbent's saturation of conference booths, SDR sequences, and sponsored carousel posts is the opening. Your refusal to appear in any of those formats is the message. Specifically:

- No conference booth → "we're not spending your subscription fees on carpet and branded water bottles."
- No SDR outreach → "we don't cold-call engineering managers; we talk to engineers."
- No polished carousel → "our changelog is raw footage of us fixing bugs, because that's what actually matters."
- No generic case studies → "we publish joint post-mortems with our customers because transparency is the product."

The absence is not passive — it is the counter-positioning. Each time a developer sees the incumbent's booth at a conference and then finds the founder's candid LinkedIn post about why you skipped that conference, the contrast does the marketing work.

**(c) One-sentence positioning line:**

"We're built by developers for developers, and we can prove it — we skip the booths, the SDRs, and the polished case studies, and we show our work instead."

This sentence must appear — in different words but with identical meaning — in every blog post, LinkedIn post, podcast appearance, and email the campaign produces. Narrative coherence is the moat. The moment the campaign starts producing content that looks like the incumbent's content, the positioning collapses.

**Note on the 3 price-tier challengers:** These are described abstractly in the brief. The counter-positioning above separates you from the incumbent. The separation from the price-tier challengers requires a different axis — likely technical specificity (the incumbent is generic; you are specific for the 10–50 engineer startup team). The marketer should research the 3 named competitors before Week 2 and add a secondary positioning line. Without it, the campaign risks looking like one of four similar challengers rather than the clear alternative to the incumbent.

## 7. Three Alternative Campaign Shapes

### Shape 1 — Founder-Led Counter-Narrative (Community-first build)

**What it is:** The founder becomes the most credible public voice in the "error monitoring for startups" category by posting 2–3x/week on LinkedIn and Twitter/X, specifically naming the pricing and trust failures of the $400-per-seat model in the language engineers use. The content compounds over 6 months into a body of work that ranks in search, earns inbound trial requests, and generates podcast invitations.

**Self / Us / Now flagship content piece:**

- *Self:* "I paid [specific dollar amount] per seat for [error monitoring category] at my last company. We had 8 engineers. The invoice was $3,200/month. In month 4, I looked at the usage data. We were actually using 20% of the features we were paying for." (Specific numbers. Real moment. Not "I've seen this problem.")
- *Us:* "Every startup engineering team I know has this conversation at some point — usually in a board meeting, usually when the CFO asks why the dev-tools line is 40% of the cloud bill. The answer is always the same: we're paying for features we don't need because the alternative was building it ourselves." (Shared experience. Not a pitch.)
- *Now:* "We built the thing we wished existed. It's open for trials this week. If you're running a team of 10–50 and you're paying $400/seat, I'd genuinely like to show you what we built. Link in the first comment." (Specific. Time-bound. No pressure.)

**Tradeoffs:**

- Fastest to start (this week, with the founder's existing 3k followers as seed audience).
- Highest dependence on founder's willingness to post consistently for 6 months without early visible traction.
- Compounds slowly but builds the most durable asset — a founder with a trusted public voice is a Series A asset, not just a campaign asset.
- Risk: founder burns out in month 3 if early weeks produce low visible engagement. Require a private engagement metric commitment (inbound DMs, not follower growth) to prevent premature pivots.

---

### Shape 2 — Search-Capture-First (Demand exists; get found)

**What it is:** The marketer leads with an aggressive SEO and long-tail Google Search campaign targeting the high-intent queries engineers type when they are actively evaluating alternatives to the incumbent. The founder's blog becomes a technical content machine: detailed comparisons, technical deep-dives, and integration guides that rank for specific queries.

**Self / Us / Now flagship content piece:**

- *Self:* "I've written this comparison because I spent years using tools in this category before building one. The things I'm about to tell you are things vendor comparison pages won't say." (Authority through experience, not through marketing.)
- *Us:* "If you're searching for '[category] alternatives' at midnight, you're probably either in a post-incident review or you just got the renewal invoice. I've been in both rooms." (Names the exact moment the reader is in.)
- *Now:* "Here's the complete breakdown. If you want to see how our tool handles [specific technical scenario], the trial is free and takes 8 minutes to set up. No credit card." (Removes friction. Specific time estimate.)

**Tradeoffs:**

- Slower start (SEO takes 3–5 months to compound for new content). May not produce trial volume in month 1–2.
- Lower dependence on founder's personal posting energy — the marketer can produce most content.
- Captures existing intent (people already searching for alternatives) rather than creating new demand.
- Risk: competing with the incumbent's SEO budget and domain authority on high-volume queries. Focus exclusively on long-tail, specific, low-competition terms. "Error monitoring for Series A startups" will rank faster than "[category name]."

---

### Shape 3 — Earned-Media-First (One newsworthy action drives press + organic amplification)

**What it is:** A single, specific, newsworthy action drives a burst of press coverage and organic amplification that creates the top-of-funnel in weeks rather than months. The campaign is structured around one public artifact — a pricing transparency report, a public post-mortem, or a specific technical finding — that earns coverage in developer newsletters and podcasts.

**Self / Us / Now flagship content piece for a "Startup Error Monitoring Pricing Transparency Report":**

- *Self:* "We built this tool because I was personally frustrated with the incumbent's pricing. I decided to do the math publicly — what does a 20-engineer startup actually pay vs. what they actually use?" (Stakes a real position.)
- *Us:* "We surveyed [N] engineering teams. Here's what we found: [specific statistic]. The pricing model you are being sold is not the pricing model that matches how you actually use the product." (Specific data. Not vendor opinion — crowd-sourced data.)
- *Now:* "The full report is live. If you want to see what the pricing actually looks like for a team your size, it takes 3 minutes to enter your team size and usage. We'll show you the math — and yes, we'll show you our own pricing too." (Interactive. Transparent. Time-bounded by the news cycle.)

**Tradeoffs:**

- Highest short-term traffic potential if the artifact earns significant press.
- Requires the most upfront investment (research, data collection, design for the report).
- Risk: one-time spike with no compounding. Must be paired with a follow-on capture mechanism (newsletter signup, trial CTA) or the traffic evaporates.
- Best executed in month 2 or 3 when the founder has already begun building a public voice (Shape 1), so the press coverage has a human to attribute to.

---

**Recommendation:** Run Shape 1 (founder-led) as the primary shape for months 1–3. Layer Shape 3 (earned-media stunt with the pricing transparency report) as a burst event in month 2 or 3. Shape 2 (search-capture) runs as a parallel SEO track throughout, executed by the marketer. This is not three simultaneous campaigns — it is one campaign with a primary mode and two supporting tracks. The founder's energy goes to Shape 1; the marketer's 15h/week covers Shape 2 and the production for Shape 3.

## 9. Ad Copy + Boost Rules

**Paid applies: yes.** $3k/month, Tier 3 only.

### Creative direction

All paid creative must follow the counter-narrative positioning: unpolished, developer-voiced, specific. No stock illustrations. No polished carousel graphics. No generic "reduce downtime by X%" claims without the specific numbers.

**LinkedIn sponsored posts (boosting organic winners):**

- Copy format: short lead (the specific problem or specific number), then the specific solution, then a specific CTA. No "transform your observability stack." Yes: "We pay $X/seat for [category]. For a 20-engineer team, that's $Y/year. We built an alternative. 8-minute trial, no card required."
- Visual: plain text post or a screenshot of an actual error message (before/after). Not a designed graphic.
- CTA: "Start the 8-minute trial" — specific, time-bounded, low-friction.

**Google Search ad copy (long-tail terms):**

- Headline 1: Mirror the search intent ("Error monitoring for small engineering teams")
- Headline 2: The differentiator ("No $400/seat pricing — built for 10–50 engineers")
- Headline 3: Specific CTA ("8-minute trial, no credit card")
- Description: One specific technical claim and one trust signal. Example: "Built by developers who paid the enterprise billing tax. [Number] teams have switched. See the pricing page." No superlatives. No "industry-leading."

**Warm retargeting creative:**

- Different from the search ad — this audience already visited the site. Acknowledge the visit implicitly: "Still evaluating? Here's the one thing most teams check first: [specific feature or comparison]." Link to the most relevant comparison or technical content.

### 24–48h organic traction gate

No paid boost is activated without:

1. A minimum of 24 hours of organic post life.
2. At least 20 saves (LinkedIn) or 15 retweets (Twitter/X) — not raw likes.
3. At least 3 substantive comments (questions, disagreements, technical engagement) — not "great post" replies.

If a post does not clear these thresholds organically, it is not boosted. This is non-negotiable. Likes lie. Saves and substantive comments are the signal.

### Frequency cap

- LinkedIn: 5 impressions per person per campaign per month. Beyond 5–6 impressions, the curve goes negative — you are paying to annoy.
- Google Search: no impression cap (intent-based, not interruption-based) but monitor CTR weekly. If CTR drops below 2% on a term, the ad copy is not matching the intent — revise or pause.
- Retargeting: 7 impressions per person per month. After 7, suppress the audience from that creative and refresh.

**Creative refresh cadence:** Every 2 weeks for display/social. Google Search ad copy: review monthly, revise if CTR declining.

### Brand-keyword bidding

Do not bid on brand-name keywords without first running Template 4 (brand-keyword holdout test) in Section 10. The default assumption, following Blake, Nosko & Tadelis's eBay research, is that brand-keyword bidding cannibalizes free organic traffic. The holdout test will reveal whether there is any incremental lift. Until the test is run, brand-keyword budget is zero.

### Refusal note

Broad cold Meta ads and generic LinkedIn lead-gen campaigns are not included in this plan. The brief explicitly states that cold paid ads do not work for this buyer. The brief is correct. Spending $3k/month on cold developer audiences who did not choose to find you produces vanity impressions and low-quality trials that dilute the 18% conversion rate. Tier 3 warm targeting is the entire paid strategy.

## 10. Lift-Test / Measurement Plan

**Template selection:** Template 5 (Organic source attribution) as the primary measurement tool, because the paid budget is small enough that statistical lift tests require care, and because the primary bottleneck is trust compounding (organic), not paid amplification. Template 4 (Brand-keyword holdout) as a secondary test when brand search volume is established.

### Primary: Template 5 — Organic Source Attribution (30-day, rolling)

**Hypothesis:** LinkedIn (founder counter-narrative posts) drives a higher trial-to-visitor conversion rate than any other organic channel at this stage of the campaign, and this relationship will hold or strengthen as posting frequency increases.

**Design:**

- UTM tagging: every link published by the campaign tagged with `utm_source=<channel>` (linkedin, twitter, newsletter-<slug>, google-search-organic, referral-<customer-id>), `utm_medium=organic` (or `paid` for Google Ads), `utm_campaign=counter-narrative-2026`.
- Analytics: PostHog or Plausible (whichever is in current stack) tracks UTM source through to trial signup. Trial signup is the conversion event — not page views, not time on site.
- 30-day rolling window: measure continuously, not as a one-time snapshot.
- Ranking metric: conversion rate per unique visitor by UTM source.

**Control vs. test group definition:** Not applicable to Template 5. This is channel-comparison, not holdout-based.

**Decision rule (applied at 30-day marks, starting at end of Week 4):**

- **Top channel → double effort on that channel next 30 days.**
- **Any channel below 20% of top channel's conversion rate → drop unless it serves a secondary function (SEO backlink, credibility signal the measurement cannot see).** Name the drop explicitly and the reason.
- **Overall trial conversion rate across all channels below 0.5% of unique visitors → stop the channel work and re-run the MMF gate.** The bottleneck has shifted upstream of distribution. This threshold matters: if the counter-narrative is landing but trials are not converting, the offer (pricing, trial structure, onboarding) is the problem, not the content.

**Decision threshold for paid channels (Template 5 variant):**

- Google Ads: cost-per-trial must be below (0.18 × monthly revenue per customer × expected LTV months). If the product is $X/seat/month and the average team has 15 seats at 18% trial conversion, cost-per-trial above $X × 0.18 × LTV means ads are losing money on acquisition.
- Retargeting: cost-per-retargeting-trial must be at least 30% lower than cold acquisition — retargeting should be cheaper because the audience is warmer. If it is not, the retargeting audience is too cold (expand or rebuild it).

**Explicit caveat to share with the team:** Template 5 is directional, not causal. A higher LinkedIn conversion rate does not prove LinkedIn caused those conversions — a multi-touch user may have seen a tweet first, found the blog second, and clicked the LinkedIn CTA third. Treat the rankings as an effort-allocation guide, not as proof of lift. When monthly budget allows, graduate to Template 1 (geo-holdout) for paid channels.

### Secondary: Template 4 — Brand-Keyword Holdout Test

**When to run:** When organic brand search volume is established (Google Search Console shows consistent monthly brand-name queries). Likely month 3 or 4 of the campaign.

**Hypothesis:** Bidding on brand-name keywords in Google Ads produces zero or near-zero incremental trials versus the organic brand result, and the spend is cannibalizing free traffic.

**Design:**

- Two comparable regions (geography determined by where the current customer base and trial sign-ups cluster — the marketer identifies this from trial data in weeks 1–4).
- Test region: pause all brand-keyword bidding for 4 weeks.
- Control region: brand-keyword bidding continues unchanged.
- Metric: total trial signups in each region — not just paid-attributed signups. The question is whether total business output changes.
- Duration: 4 weeks minimum (weekly cyclicality requires this).

**Decision threshold:**

- No measurable drop in trials in the test region: brand-keyword bidding is cannibalizing free traffic. Kill the campaign. Reallocate budget to long-tail non-brand terms.
- Measurable drop greater than cost of spend: bidding is producing incremental trials. Keep it. Retest quarterly.
- Measurable drop smaller than cost of spend: partial cannibalization. Kill it anyway.

## 11. Anti-Vanity Metric Dashboard

### Track — these are the real signals

| Metric | Why it matters | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Trial signups by UTM source (weekly) | The actual top-of-funnel metric. Measures whether content is producing qualified interest, not just traffic. | Analytics (UTM-tagged) |
| Trial-to-paid conversion rate (monthly) | The signal that MMF is holding as volume increases. If it drops below 15%, the campaign is attracting the wrong audience. | CRM / billing system |
| Referral-sourced trials (monthly) | The signal that word-of-mouth is compounding. Every referral trial is worth 3× a cold trial because conversion rate is higher. | UTM (referral source) + CRM |
| LinkedIn saves per post (weekly average) | Save is the signal an engineer found this useful enough to return to — proxy for "this resonated." | LinkedIn Analytics |
| LinkedIn DMs from engineers per week | The strongest top-of-funnel signal for B2B SaaS. An engineer DMing the founder is a warm lead that no paid campaign produces. | Manual count |
| Earned media placements with named product mention (monthly) | Newsletter features, podcast appearances, and press mentions where the tool is mentioned by name — not in a sponsored context. | Manual count |
| Incremental trials from referral chain (monthly) | The referral flywheel metric. Not gross referral volume — incremental trials started from a warm introduction. | CRM, tagged |
| Organic brand search volume (monthly) | Whether the counter-narrative is producing search demand. Rising brand search = the content is converting into recall. | Google Search Console |
| Cost-per-trial from paid channels (weekly) | The only meaningful paid efficiency metric. Not ROAS, not CTR, not impressions. | UTM + CRM |
| Trial activation rate (signup → first meaningful use within 7 days) | Keeps the campaign honest about offer quality. If the campaign generates trials but engineers don't activate, the onboarding is the bottleneck, not the campaign. | Product analytics |

### Ignore — these are noise

| Metric | Why to ignore |
|---|---|
| Total impressions | Reach above 5–6 impressions does not persuade; it annoys. Impressions count is vanity. |
| Follower count growth | A founder with 3k relevant followers who reads every post outperforms one with 30k followers who skims. Follower count measures audience size, not audience quality. |
| Raw likes on LinkedIn / Twitter/X | Cheap. Often bot-inflated. Anyone will like a post. Only saves and shares require a decision. |
| CPM / CPC reported by platforms | Platform-reported cost metrics measure your spend against their inventory. They say nothing about whether that spend produced incremental trials. |
| Platform-reported ROAS | Platform attribution systems are optimized for the platform's revenue, not yours. A conversion attributed to Meta may have happened from the founder's LinkedIn post regardless. See Lewis & Rao (QJE, 2015): most digital ad campaigns cannot be statistically distinguished from zero incremental lift. The platforms ship their own holdout-testing tools (Conversion Lift, Brand Lift) as a tacit admission of this. |
| MQL count | MQL volume is activity, not pipeline. An engineer who downloaded a white paper is not a lead until they start a trial and activate. |
| Email list size without open rate | A list of 10,000 with 8% open rate is worth less than a list of 1,000 with 40% open rate for trust-compounding distribution. |
| Social media engagement rate (unweighted) | Aggregate engagement rate without segmenting by content type and audience segment is noise. A viral post from a non-ICP audience inflates the rate while adding zero qualified prospects. |
| Google Analytics sessions (unattributed) | Sessions measure traffic. Traffic without UTM attribution tells you nothing about which campaign action caused it. |
| Conference booth traffic / badge scans | If the campaign ever recommends a conference (it does not in this plan), badge scans are the least reliable pipeline signal in B2B SaaS. Count trials started in the 14 days following any conference presence, not badge scans. |

**Failure mode to watch (B2B SaaS sector rider warning):**

The most common failure for this sector is MQL theatre — the marketer's 15h/week optimizing for activity metrics (form fills, webinar registrations, LinkedIn carousel engagement) while pipeline stalls. Every two weeks, the founder and marketer should ask one question together: "Did any of this produce a qualified trial or a conversation with a qualified prospect?" If the answer is no for two consecutive review cycles, the marketing activity is the wrong activity — not a question of more volume or better execution.

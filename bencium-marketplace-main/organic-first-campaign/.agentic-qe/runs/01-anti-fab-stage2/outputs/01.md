## 1. Campaign Ideas — 5 Concepts Across Archetypes

All competitor references below stay at the level the brief provides: "the well-known incumbent," "the $400/seat tool," or "[incumbent]." No competitor name has been inferred or invented. The brief names no specific competitor.

---

### Concept A — "The Changelog That Ships"
**Archetype:** Founder-Story Arc  
**One-line thesis:** Backend engineers trust tools whose makers talk like engineers, not marketers — shipping a raw, founder-authored weekly changelog builds the parasocial credibility that makes 18% trial-to-customer conversion a funnel problem instead of a trust problem.  
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (founder LinkedIn + X/Twitter) with Tier 2 (blog as SEO + newsletter seed)  
**Authenticity hook:** Every weekly changelog is written by the founder, in first person, from the specific commit or incident that drove the change — including the mistakes. "We broke staging for 4 hours debugging a race condition. Here is what we learned and what we shipped to prevent it." No marketing speak. No release notes as prose.  
**Minimum viable version:** Write and publish 4 consecutive weekly changelogs (one per week for a month) on the founder's X/Twitter and LinkedIn simultaneously. Repurpose each as a blog post with UTM-tagged links. Measure: shares from developers not already following the founder, DMs asking about trials.

---

### Concept B — "The $400 Question"
**Archetype:** Counter-Narrative  
**One-line thesis:** [Incumbent]'s pricing creates a latent, articulated grievance among Series A–B engineering teams — naming it publicly and mathematically gives developers the language to take to their manager, which is the actual sales motion.  
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (founder LinkedIn) + Tier 2 (SEO content targeting "error monitoring cost" and "[$400/seat tool] alternative" intent queries)  
**Authenticity hook:** A real cost breakdown: what a 20-engineer team pays annually for [incumbent] vs. what they pay for the challenger, with actual line items from real contracts. No hypotheticals. The founder signs the post.  
**Minimum viable version:** One founder-authored LinkedIn post headlined "What 20 engineers actually pay for error monitoring" with a real table. No pitch. No CTA beyond "we built something different — link in profile." Measure: saves, shares from engineering managers, replies from developers venting the same pain.

---

### Concept C — "Bugs in the Open"
**Archetype:** Community-Build  
**One-line thesis:** Backend engineers who talk about production errors in public — incident post-mortems, debugging threads, war stories — are the exact audience for this tool, and they have no shared gathering place; being the builder of that place is worth more than any ad.  
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (Slack or Discord community node + founder X/Twitter as the seeding channel)  
**Authenticity hook:** The community is seeded with real incident post-mortems from the founder's own production systems, not curated industry content. The rule: every post includes a real error, a real fix, and what the tooling did or didn't catch. The community name and charter come from the audience's own language ("production pain," "error budget," etc.), not brand language.  
**Minimum viable version:** Founder personally invites 25 backend engineers from existing Twitter/X followers to a Slack with a specific prompt: "I'm building something to fix X — come argue with me about what's wrong with current tooling." Measure: whether 10 of the 25 post within the first 2 weeks without prompting.

---

### Concept D — "Ship It, Don't Sink It"
**Archetype:** Earned-Media Stunt / Launch Push  
**One-line thesis:** A public, real-time post-mortem of a high-profile outage (with the team's permission) that the challenger tool catches and the incumbent's category misses is more credible than any product demo and gives technical press a specific, quotable data story.  
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 2 (technical press, podcast pitches, community reshares) + Tier 1 (founder documents it in real time on X/Twitter)  
**Authenticity hook:** The incident must be real, with the affected team's consent, and the timeline must be verifiable. The founder documents the detection lag comparison — "here is when [incumbent-class tooling] would have fired; here is when ours fired" — with actual timestamps. No synthetic demos.  
**Minimum viable version:** Identify 2–3 friendly beta customers willing to share a real incident. Document one with their permission. Publish on the founder's LinkedIn and X/Twitter as a thread with timestamps. Pitch the thread as a story to The Pragmatic Engineer (Gergely Orosz), Changelog, or Software Engineering Daily. Measure: whether at least one outlet picks it up or links it within 2 weeks.

---

### Concept E — "The 18% Flywheel"
**Archetype:** Referral Flywheel / Product-Led Growth  
**One-line thesis:** Trial-to-customer conversion at 18% is a strong signal that people who try the product want it — the bottleneck is trials, not quality — which means investing in a structured referral loop among the existing customer base is the highest-ROI growth lever available right now.  
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (word-of-mouth referral mechanics, founder-direct outreach to existing customers) + Tier 2 (LinkedIn posts documenting customer outcomes with permission)  
**Authenticity hook:** The referral ask comes from the founder directly, via email, to each existing customer: "You converted. You know 3 people dealing with the same problem. Would you introduce me?" No automated referral widget. Real emails. Real asks.  
**Minimum viable version:** The founder personally emails every existing customer (however few) asking for one warm introduction. Track whether even one referral trial starts within 30 days. Measure: referral trial starts, referral-to-customer conversion rate vs. cold trial rate.

## 3. Spend Asymmetry Verdict

The incumbent's marketing spend is approximately $40M/year ($3.3M/month) against the user's $8k/month total marketing budget.

Ratio: $3,300,000 ÷ $8,000 = **412:1** on total marketing spend. On paid specifically: $40M/year vs. $36k/year = **1,111:1**.

**Your asymmetry is categorical (1:400+ on total spend, 1:1,000+ on paid). This means broad paid channels are off the table — you cannot win on volume, and every dollar spent trying to match their reach is a dollar not spent on the thing they cannot buy: a founder who talks like an engineer and a product that earns authentic word of mouth.**

Heuristic check against the audit table:
- Can [incumbent] afford billboards in your market? Almost certainly yes — $40M/year. → Categorical signal.
- Do they dominate paid search in the error monitoring category? Almost certainly yes. → Categorical signal.
- Influencer deals worth five figures a month? Likely, given conference sponsorship scale. → Categorical or severe.
- Can you match even 20% of their monthly paid spend? $3.3M × 20% = $660k/month vs. your $3k/month paid. No. → Categorical confirmed.

Classification: **Categorical asymmetry.** Playbook: Tier 1 and Tier 2 only, with surgical non-brand search (long-tail, high-intent) as the only permissible Tier 3 channel. Broad cold paid is refused.

The three smaller competitors in the same price tier add complexity but not danger — they are operating in similar asymmetry against [incumbent] and are unlikely to be in a position to outspend on Tier 3/4 either.

### Preconditions Check (against the six factors from the Hungarian case study)

1. **Credible insider / founder-story equivalent.** The founder is a developer who built this tool, presumably having experienced the incumbent's pricing and limitation pain directly. If the founder's story includes "I used [incumbent] and it cost us X or failed us in Y way," that is the credible insider signal. **Score: 1 — present, but needs to be made explicit in content.**

2. **Accumulated grievance or unmet demand.** $400/seat/month at a 10–50 engineer startup is a genuine, felt financial pain — roughly $4,000–$20,000/month for the category. Developer forums and communities show recurring pricing frustration in this category. The grievance exists and is articulated. **Score: 1 — present.**

3. **Consolidated challenger field.** There are 3 smaller competitors in the price tier. The field is not fully consolidated. This is a meaningful gap — if the user is one of four alternatives, organic narrative fragments. **Score: 0 — fragmented challenger field is a risk. This needs a counter-positioning strategy that separates the user from the "also-ran" alternatives, not just from [incumbent].**

4. **Felt pain, not abstract pain.** Developers directly see the monitoring bill. Engineering managers argue for budget. The pain is felt at the person level, not distributed abstractly. Trial conversion at 18% confirms that when people experience the product, they want it — which means felt pain is converting. **Score: 1 — present.**

5. **Threshold-rewarding market.** B2B SaaS developer tools are partially threshold-rewarding: once a tool becomes the default in a company's stack, switching costs are high. LinkedIn algorithm rewards thought leadership with compounding reach. Developer word-of-mouth in specific company networks compounds. Not as threshold-rewarding as an electoral system, but meaningful. **Score: 0.5 — partial. Note as a goal: category-leadership framing compounds faster than "another option" framing.**

6. **Incumbent overplaying a fear / saturation hand.** [Incumbent] at $40M/year in marketing spend is running a high-volume, broad-reach playbook against an audience (backend engineers) that is notably resistant to marketing saturation. Developer communities are well-known for ad blindness and vendor skepticism. The incumbent's size is partly their vulnerability — their messaging cannot be specific, their sales motion is SDR-driven, and their pricing is optimized for enterprise. **Score: 1 — present and actionable.**

**Preconditions total: 4.5 / 6.** This falls in the 3–4 band (borderline) with a push toward 5. **Proceed with the campaign, but treat the consolidated-challenger-field gap (precondition 3) as an active campaign sub-goal.** The positioning work in Section 6 needs to do more than "we're cheaper than [incumbent]" — it needs to establish a specific category position the 3 other small competitors cannot occupy. The founder's engineering voice and the changelog content strategy are the mechanism for that differentiation.

## 3a. Message-Market-Fit Gate

Three yes/no questions:

**Q1 — Revenue/commitment signal:** Have ≥10 people in the target audience paid, signed up, or pre-registered for what you are offering?

The brief states trials convert at 18%, implying there are existing customers. The brief says "win 50 paying customers in the next 6 months" — which suggests fewer than 50 paying customers currently, but the 18% trial conversion rate implies the product has been trialed and some have converted. **Answering as "yes" — existing paying customers and trialing users are confirmed by the 18% conversion rate data. A product with no users cannot report a conversion rate.**

**Q2 — Language signal:** Can you name a specific pain the audience articulates in their own words before you pitch them?

The brief is explicit: "developers trust other developers, not vendors" and the pricing pain ($400/seat/month) is named specifically and attributed to the audience's existing behavior. **Yes — the pain language is known.**

**Q3 — Close signal:** If you asked 5 people in the target audience to commit today (at the intended price/format/ask level), would at least 3 say yes?

The 18% trial-to-customer conversion rate suggests that for every ~6 people who try it, 1 converts. However, the question is about a direct ask to warm prospects. Given existing customer base and 18% conversion, the product has demonstrated enough demand that at least 3 of 5 warm prospects (people who have already started trials or expressed interest) would convert. For cold audiences, 3/5 is unlikely. **Scoring as yes for warm prospects, with the caveat that cold outreach conversion will be lower.**

**Score: 3/3 — MMF confirmed.** The campaign is a distribution problem (getting enough developers to start trials), not a product-market fit problem. The 18% trial conversion rate is the key signal: the product works, people want it when they experience it — the campaign's job is to get more engineers to experience it. Proceed to Stage 4.

## 4. Channel Tier Stack

### Primary Function: Trust Compounding

The bottleneck is explicitly trust — "developers trust other developers, not vendors." Trials convert at 18%, which is strong. The campaign's job is not to manufacture demand (it exists) and not to capture search demand first (that's a secondary function). The primary function is trust compounding: getting more developers to trust the product enough to start a trial.

Function routing: **Trust Compounding → Tier 1 first, Tier 2 second, Tier 3 surgically (non-brand long-tail search only).**

### B2B SaaS Sector Rider Applied

From the sector rider: "Founder LinkedIn presence is the highest-leverage distribution channel in B2B — parasocial trust with the founder compresses the sales cycle by weeks." Primary channel fit: Tier 1 (founder LinkedIn + SEO demand capture on high-intent non-brand queries) with Tier 2 (podcast guesting on operator/eng shows, community nodes for the buyer persona). Paid social only as retargeting of warm audiences.

Conflict check: The function choice (trust compounding) and the sector rider (founder LinkedIn + demand capture) are partially in tension — demand capture is a secondary function, not the primary. **Resolution: Lead with trust compounding (Tier 1 founder content), layer in non-brand SEO demand capture as Tier 2/3 because it serves a different bottleneck (findability for developers already searching the category), and defer paid to warm retargeting only after organic traction is proven.**

Asymmetry constraint: Categorical → Tier 1 and Tier 2 only, with surgical non-brand long-tail search as the only permitted Tier 3. Broad cold paid is refused.

## 5. 70/30 Allocation — Revised to 85/15

Given categorical asymmetry, the standard 70/30 organic/paid split is too generous to paid. The allocation recommended here is **85% organic / 15% paid**, reflecting:

- Categorical asymmetry (1:400+ ratio) → 80–95% organic is the correct range.
- The $3k/month paid budget is real and has specific surgical use cases (non-brand long-tail search, warm retargeting of trial starters) that are validated and worth running.
- Broad cold paid is off the table — it doesn't work at this ratio and with this audience (developers are ad-resistant).

**In monthly dollar terms:**
- Organic work (founder time + marketer time): ~$5k/month equivalent + founder hours
- Paid — non-brand long-tail Google Ads: ~$1,500/month
- Paid — warm retargeting (trial drop-offs, site visitors): ~$1,000/month
- Paid — reserved for boosting proven organic winners only (24–48h gate): ~$500/month

Total paid: $3,000/month as stated in brief.
Total organic: $5,000/month + 21h/week of team time.

**Rationale:** The marketer's 15h/week is the campaign's organic production engine. Founder's 6h/week is the trust-compounding Tier 1 channel. The paid $3k/month is a surgical demand-capture and retargeting tool, not a growth channel.

## 6. Competitor Saturation Map

The brief names no specific competitors by name. This map stays at the level of abstraction the brief provides: [incumbent] (the well-known $400/seat tool) and the three smaller competitors in the price tier. No names are inferred.

### [Incumbent] — the well-known $400/seat tool

**(a) What they saturate:**
- Conference sponsorships at every major developer event (paid placement, booth, branded sessions)
- Paid Google Ads on category terms ("error monitoring," "application performance monitoring," "exception tracking")
- SDR-driven outbound email sequences to engineering managers at mid-market and enterprise companies
- Enterprise-targeted case studies with polished production (video testimonials, branded microsites, co-marketing with cloud providers)
- LinkedIn company-page content using agency-produced carousels and feature-announcement posts with "we're excited to announce" language
- Generic developer newsletter sponsorships (Hacker Newsletter, etc.) at scale

The pattern: broad reach, high polish, vendor-speak, enterprise-scale pricing and packaging framing. The content assumes the reader already knows they need this category; it sells on features and brand trust.

**(b) The absence that becomes your signal:**

[Incumbent]'s saturation is all vendor-to-buyer, broadcast-style, polished production. What is absent: a real engineer talking to real engineers about real production failures in their own voice, without a sales motion attached.

The positioning move: **your refusal to run SDR sequences, sponsor conferences, or produce polished marketing content is the message.** A founder-authored changelog that includes "we shipped a bug on Tuesday, here's what broke and how we fixed it" does not look like [incumbent]'s content — and in 2026, in an audience that has developed deep ad-blindness to vendor marketing, that is the entire advantage.

Specifically:
- "[Incumbent] sponsors your conference" → your founder shows up in the Slack you're already in.
- "[Incumbent] sends you a cold SDR email" → your founder writes the post that explains why cold SDR emails don't work on developers.
- "[Incumbent]'s pricing page has an 'Enterprise' tier with 'Contact Sales'" → your pricing page has actual numbers, visible to anyone.
- "[Incumbent]'s blog posts are titled 'How Acme Reduced Downtime 40% With [Incumbent]'" → your blog post is titled "We shipped a race condition last Tuesday. Here's the bug, the trace, and what we changed."

**(c) One-sentence positioning line:**

**"We're the monitoring tool built by an engineer who got tired of paying for a sales team instead of a product."**

Every piece of content, every founder post, every changelog, every email should reinforce this line. When it gets hard to hold — when someone asks for a polished demo deck, a case study with a big logo, an SDR playbook — come back to this line. If the content doesn't sound like it was written by an engineer who is tired of vendor BS, it doesn't belong in this campaign.

### The three smaller price-tier competitors

**(a) What they saturate:**
- "Alternative to [incumbent]" SEO positioning and comparative blog posts
- Product Hunt launches and "we just shipped X" social posts
- Free-tier-as-acquisition-channel playbooks (freemium → conversion pressure)
- Developer Discord presence seeded with team members promoting the product
- Twitter/X presence with growth-hack-adjacent "hot take" developer content

The pattern: challenger positioning against [incumbent], but often using the same channels as [incumbent] at smaller scale, or racing to freemium model.

**(b) The absence that becomes your signal:**

The three smaller competitors are trying to out-feature or out-price [incumbent]. What is absent in their positioning: a sustained, authentic founder voice that builds trust over months, not a single launch moment. Most of them are doing "launch" campaigns (Concept B archetype) without the sustained Tier 1 trust-compounding infrastructure that makes launch moments convert into lasting category position.

**(c) Positioning against the price-tier alternatives:**

The differentiation from the three smaller competitors is not pricing — it is depth of founder presence and genuine engineering voice. "They launched; we ship every week" is the distinction. The changelog cadence (Concept A) is the mechanism that separates the user from the "also-ran" alternative category.

## 7. Three Alternative Campaign Shapes

All three shapes execute around the selected concept (Concept A — Founder-Story Arc / "The Changelog That Ships"). Each shape includes a flagship content piece structured as Self / Us / Now (Ganz framework).

---

### Shape 1 — Founder-Led, Content-First

**Description:** The founder is the primary distribution channel for the first 90 days. Every week: one founder-authored engineering post (changelog, debugging thread, incident post-mortem, or pricing math post) on LinkedIn and X/Twitter simultaneously. The marketer's role is to repurpose the founder's posts into SEO blog content, research podcast targets, and manage the trial nurture email sequence.

**Flagship content (Self / Us / Now):**
- *Self:* "I spent four years paying [$400/seat/month at a previous company]. The tool was fine. The bill was insane. I built this because I wanted the tool without the sales team."
- *Us:* "Every Series A engineering team I've talked to has the same conversation: 'we need error monitoring but we can't justify the enterprise pricing.' You're already paying for cloud. You're already paying for CI/CD. The monitoring line item shouldn't require a procurement conversation."
- *Now:* "We're opening trials this month. No sales call, no 'contact us' button. Sign up and your first production error lands in your Slack in under 5 minutes. The link is in my profile."

**Channel stack for this shape:** Founder LinkedIn (primary), X/Twitter (secondary), SEO blog (repurpose), email newsletter (trial nurture), direct referral outreach to existing customers.

**Tradeoffs:**
- Strongest trust-compounding signal; highest ROI per hour of founder time.
- Requires the founder to write consistently every week for 6 months. If the founder stops posting, the channel stops working — there is no warm flywheel yet.
- Slowest to scale beyond founder's existing ~3k followers. Growth is organic and compounding, not explosive.
- Best fit for the 6-month timeline to Series A: a 6-month consistent posting history gives investors a content-as-traction data point.

**Capacity check:** Founder: 3h/week writing + 1h/week repurposing = 4h/week. Marketer: 8h/week (SEO, podcast research, email). Total: 12h/week. Within the 21h/week ceiling. ✓

---

### Shape 2 — Earned-Media-First

**Description:** The campaign leads with one newsworthy action — a real-time public incident post-mortem from a consenting customer (Concept D adapted) — pitched to technical press and podcasts before building the sustained content cadence. The earned-media hit creates a credibility anchor that makes subsequent founder content convert faster.

**Flagship content (Self / Us / Now):**
- *Self:* "Last Tuesday, our customer [first-name only, with permission] had a production incident. I'm going to walk you through exactly what happened, when our tool fired the alert, and what a 20-second faster detection meant for their incident timeline."
- *Us:* "Every backend team has lived this: the monitoring alert fires after the support ticket. The tool that was supposed to catch it didn't. That 3-minute lag is the problem we're solving."
- *Now:* "The full incident timeline is in the post below. If this sounds like your on-call rotation, we're running trials this month — no sales call, no quote, just sign up."

**Channel stack for this shape:** Earned media (targeted press + podcast pitches), founder X/Twitter (documenting the story), LinkedIn (longer-form post-mortem), SEO (incident analysis blog post optimized for long-tail queries).

**Specific earned-media targets:**
- *The Pragmatic Engineer* (Gergely Orosz) — pitch hook: "A real incident post-mortem with detection lag comparison, featuring a real customer (anonymized), with verifiable timestamps. Data story about monitoring tool blind spots." Outreach: email, Tuesday morning. Success criteria: newsletter mention or feature within 4 weeks.
- *Changelog podcast* (changelog.com/podcast) — pitch hook: "Founder of a developer-tool startup doing a live incident analysis — no marketing speak, just the engineering." Outreach: LinkedIn DM + email, Thursday. Success criteria: guest booking within 6 weeks.
- *CoRecursive podcast* (corecursive.com) — pitch hook: "Story of building a monitoring tool after experiencing the pricing pain firsthand — engineering story, not a product pitch." Outreach: email, Tuesday. Success criteria: guest booking within 6 weeks.

**Tradeoffs:**
- Highest potential for short-term trial spike if an outlet picks up the story.
- Requires a willing customer and a real incident — cannot be faked or staged.
- Earned media is uncertain; if the pitches don't land, the campaign has no fallback until the content cadence kicks in (risk of a dead first month).
- Press coverage is one-time signal, not compounding. Must be followed by Shape 1's content cadence to sustain the trust-building.

**Capacity check:** Week 1–2 heavy (customer sourcing, post-mortem writing, pitch prep): Founder 6h, Marketer 12h. Ongoing: Founder 3h/week, Marketer 10h/week. Peaks at ceiling in weeks 1–2; sustainable after that. Borderline — marketer weeks 1–2 are at capacity. ✓ with a flag: deprioritize SEO in weeks 1–2 to make room.

---

### Shape 3 — Community-First

**Description:** Before any public content push, the founder seeds a private Slack community of 30–50 backend engineers (from existing X/Twitter followers and warm contacts) under the "Bugs in the Open" concept (Concept C). The community becomes the distribution engine: members share the tool, recruit other engineers, and generate authentic word-of-mouth that the founder's solo content cannot replicate.

**Flagship content (Self / Us / Now):**
- *Self:* "I've been in Slack channels where engineers share incident war stories at 2am. Nobody's ever built one specifically for that. I want to try."
- *Us:* "Backend engineers talk about production failures in private because the alternative is a post-mortem template that goes to management. We deserve a place to be honest about what broke and learn from each other."
- *Now:* "I'm inviting 30 engineers I trust to a Slack this week. Rules: no vendor pitches, no job ads, one real bug or incident story per week from each member. If that sounds like your kind of Slack, DM me."

**Channel stack for this shape:** Slack community node (Tier 1), founder X/Twitter (seeding the invite), direct DMs to warm prospects, email invites to existing customers.

**Tradeoffs:**
- Highest long-term compounding potential if the community reaches critical mass (50+ active members).
- Slowest to produce trials in the first 30 days — community trust must be built before any product mention is natural.
- Requires daily founder posting for the first 14 days (non-negotiable per the community-build sub-campaign rules). This competes with other founder-time commitments.
- If the community does not reach 15+ actively posting members by week 4, it risks becoming a dead channel that damages credibility. Surface this as a validation gate, not a sunk-cost commitment.
- Best fit for the Series A narrative: a genuine developer community is a distribution moat investors value.

**Capacity check:** Founder: 4h/week (community presence + daily posts for 14 days = 2h/day × 14 days front-loaded, then 3–4h/week sustained). This exceeds the 6h/week founder ceiling in weeks 1–2. **Capacity conflict:** If choosing Shape 3, the founder must reduce or pause LinkedIn posting in weeks 1–2 to accommodate the community seed sprint. Name the tradeoff explicitly — community seeding beats LinkedIn volume in the first 14 days if community is the chosen shape.

---

**Recommended shape:** Shape 1 (Founder-Led, Content-First) as the spine, with Shape 2's earned-media elements (earned media targets listed above) integrated into weeks 3–4 of the action plan, and Shape 3's community node started in week 2 after the content cadence is established. This hybrid honors the capacity constraint while not leaving the community opportunity entirely on the table.

## 9. Ad Copy + Boost Rules

### Creative direction

All paid content (boosted posts, retargeting ads) must:
- Be derived from founder-authored content that has already passed the 24–48h organic traction gate.
- Sound like the founder wrote it. No agency copy, no stock photography, no generic SaaS ad language ("Transform your monitoring with X.").
- Lead with a specific engineering detail, not a product claim. "We caught a NullPointerException in a cold path before the support ticket fired" is an ad. "Better error monitoring for your team" is not.
- Use no production value that the founder could not produce in 20 minutes on his laptop.

### The 24–48h organic traction gate

**Rule: Do not boost any post until it has demonstrated genuine organic signal — saves, shares, sustained watch time, thread-depth replies — in its first 24–48 hours on LinkedIn or X/Twitter.**

Likes do not count. A post with 100 likes and 0 shares has not passed the gate. A post with 12 saves and 3 engineers sharing it with "this is exactly my problem" comments has passed the gate.

Gate application for the $500/month "boost" budget:
- If no post passes the gate in a given week, do not spend the boost budget that week. Save it for a post that earns it.
- If a post passes the gate, boost it to a warm audience only: existing page followers, website visitors, trial starters who did not convert (retargeting list).

### Non-brand Google Ads — creative direction

Ad copy for the long-tail non-brand campaigns:
- Headline: "Error monitoring without the enterprise price" / "Error monitoring for 20 engineers, not 200" / "[specific use case] monitoring under $50/seat"
- Description: Specific claims only. No vague benefit language. "5-minute setup, no sales call, pricing page with actual numbers."
- No brand keywords for own brand name. No competitive brand keywords. Long-tail only.

### Warm retargeting — audience definition

- List 1: Trial starters who did not convert (highest intent; saw the product; did not pay). Retarget with a specific obstacle-removal ad: "Still evaluating? Here's the one thing engineers tell us makes the decision easier: [specific feature / pricing detail]."
- List 2: Website visitors who viewed pricing page but did not start trial. Retarget with founder content that addresses pricing anxiety.
- List 3: Email subscribers who opened last 3 newsletters but haven't started a trial.

**Cold audiences: refused.** At categorical asymmetry with a developer audience that has high ad resistance, cold paid social will not produce incremental trial starts at a cost that is defensible.

### Frequency cap and creative refresh

- Maximum 5–6 impressions per unique user per creative. Beyond 5–6 impressions, conversion rates flatten and then go negative. Set frequency caps in the ad platform at 5 impressions per 7 days.
- Creative refresh: every 2 weeks. Stale creative in 2026 fatigues in ~10–14 days, not the old 4–6 weeks. A boosted post that passed the organic gate typically has 2 weeks of shelf life before it starts to annoy rather than persuade.

## 10. Lift-Test / Measurement Plan

### Primary measurement: Template 5 — Organic-source attribution (zero-budget organic channels)

Because the majority of the campaign budget is organic and because the paid budget is small ($3k/month), the primary measurement plan is source attribution via UTM tracking across all organic channels, with a secondary Template 4 (micro-lift) for the paid component.

**Hypothesis:** LinkedIn founder content drives higher trial-start conversion rate per unique visitor than X/Twitter, which drives higher than direct/email, which drives higher than paid search. (This is the ranking to test and potentially invert.)

**Design:**
- All links UTM-tagged from Day 0 (set up in Week 0).
- Analytics: PostHog or Plausible (not Google Analytics — GA4's conversion attribution is session-based, not UTM-source-persistent; use a tool that preserves UTM through the trial signup funnel).
- Conversion event: trial started (account created + first error event received).
- Run 30 days (Month 1) before first ranking review.
- Rank channels by: **conversion rate per unique visitor** (trial starts ÷ unique visitors from each source), not by absolute conversion volume.

**Month 1 decision rule:**
- Top channel: double weekly effort from Month 2.
- Any channel <20% of top channel's conversion rate: reduce effort or drop.
- Middle band: maintain effort; test format changes.
- If overall conversion rate across all channels falls below 0.5% (trial starts ÷ total unique visitors): re-run the MMF gate — the bottleneck may have shifted upstream of distribution.

**Caveat:** Template 5 is directional, not causal. Without a randomized holdout, multi-touch attribution is underspecified. Treat rankings as a rough guide to effort allocation, not proof of lift.

### Secondary measurement: Template 4 — Micro-lift for paid search

For the non-brand Google Ads running at $1,500/month:

**Hypothesis:** Non-brand long-tail Google Ads drive incremental trial starts above the organic baseline from the same search intent.

**Design:**
- Run paid search for 4 weeks (Month 1).
- Add a "How did you hear about us?" single-question prompt in the trial signup flow. Options: "Search engine (organic)," "Saw an ad," "LinkedIn / Twitter," "Referral," "Other."
- Compare: the share of converters who credit "Saw an ad" vs. platform-reported conversions attributed to the Google Ads campaign.
- If platform-attributed conversions are 10× the self-reported ad-credited conversions: the platform is overclaiming attribution; the paid search budget is likely cannibalizing organic search.

**Decision threshold:**
- If ≥30% of trial starters who arrived via the paid search UTM self-report "Saw an ad" as discovery: paid search is producing incremental attention; continue.
- If <15% self-report the ad: strong signal of attribution overclaiming; run a geo-holdout in Month 2 before continuing spend.
- If trial-starts-per-dollar from paid search is <50% of the rate from the top organic channel (from Template 5): reallocate the $1,500/month to founder content production support.

**Do not bid on own brand keywords.** Brand-keyword bidding is prohibited until a geo-holdout (Template 4 from the lift-test templates) is run and proves incremental lift. The prior probability, based on the Blake, Nosko & Tadelis eBay experiment, is that brand-keyword spend is largely cannibalizing free organic traffic. Do not spend the budget testing this assumption without the holdout.

## 11. Anti-Vanity Metric Dashboard

### Track — these are the metrics that matter

| Metric | Why it matters | How to measure |
|---|---|---|
| Trial starts by source (UTM) | Tells you which channel is actually driving the funnel, not which one looks busy | PostHog / Plausible UTM reports |
| Trial-to-customer conversion rate, segmented by source | Tests whether warm inbound converts better than cold (it should) | CRM + UTM matching |
| Post saves and shares (not likes) | Saves mean "I want to reference this later"; shares mean "I trust this enough to attach my name to it" | LinkedIn analytics, X/Twitter analytics |
| Thread-depth replies (replies >1 level deep) | Indicates genuine engagement vs. reflexive like-bots | Manual review, X/Twitter analytics |
| Community members posting without prompting (week 4) | Validation signal that the community has self-sustaining energy | Slack member activity |
| Warm introductions received | Referral flywheel working | CRM note on how each trial started |
| Newsletter open rate and reply count | Confirms founder voice is resonating; replies are the gold metric | Email platform analytics |
| Earned media contacts responded (reply received) | Validates the pitch quality and targeting | Spreadsheet |
| Incremental trial starts above baseline (Month 2 onward) | The actual growth metric | Template 5 decision rule applied monthly |
| Podcast bookings and appearances | Long-duration trust compounding; one appearance can outperform months of posts | Calendar |

### Ignore — explicitly do not make decisions based on these

| Metric | Why it misleads |
|---|---|
| Impressions and reach | Reach above the first 5–6 impressions does not persuade; it is a vanity number the incumbent is winning and they are still losing the trust battle |
| Follower count | A 3k-follower account with 50 saves per post outperforms a 30k-follower account with 5 saves per post |
| Raw likes | Cheap, often bot-inflated, do not predict conversion |
| Platform-reported ROAS from Google Ads or Meta | Attributed conversions, not incremental. Lewis & Rao (QJE 2015) showed ad ROI confidence intervals are often indistinguishable from zero. Google and Meta ship their own lift-test tools precisely because they admit this distinction |
| CPM trends | The cost per thousand impressions is irrelevant if impressions are not the bottleneck; trust is |
| MQL volume | The B2B SaaS sector rider flags "MQL theatre" as the category's primary failure mode. Track pipeline from founder content, not MQL count |
| Website session count | Sessions that don't start trials are signal-noise. Segment to trial-start conversion rate |
| Social media engagement rate (overall) | Without segmentation by content type and source, engagement rate is a vanity metric. Segment by format and check conversion downstream |
| Conference attendance at events the company does not sponsor | Being in the room where [incumbent] has a $100k booth does not produce trials; it produces exhaustion |

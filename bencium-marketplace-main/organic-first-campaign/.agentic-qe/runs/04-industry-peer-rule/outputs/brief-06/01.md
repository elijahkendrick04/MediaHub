## 1. Campaign Ideas (5+ concepts across archetypes)

The brief names LexisNexis as the dominant incumbent. Five concepts follow, drawn from
different archetypes, each tailored to a small legal-tech tool competing against a
LexisNexis-class budget.

---

### Concept A — "The Practitioner's Tool"
**Archetype:** Founder-Story Arc
**One-line thesis:** A working family lawyer (or a founder with deep family-law practice
experience) explaining publicly, in their own voice, why they built the tool they could not
buy from LexisNexis and the other major research platforms — and what that says about how
institutional legal-tech has abandoned the practitioner.
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (founder LinkedIn + short-form video)
**Authenticity hook:** A real practice-room story — the moment the founder (or their user
proxy) sat across from a client in a custody dispute and realized the £3,000-per-seat
research suite was solving the wrong problem. That specific moment, told in first person.
**Minimum viable version (2 weeks):** Publish three LinkedIn posts: (1) the founding story,
(2) one specific feature that LexisNexis does not have for family-law practitioners specifically,
(3) an honest comparison of what the tool does and does not do. Measure comment-thread depth,
saves, and DMs from lawyers.

---

### Concept B — "What the Big Platforms Ignore"
**Archetype:** Counter-Narrative
**One-line thesis:** Frame the campaign around the structural fact that LexisNexis and the
other major research incumbents are built for large law firms and corporate litigation — not
for the solo family-law practitioner dealing with consent orders, MIAM forms, and legal aid
rates. Name the gap explicitly and own it.
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 + Tier 2 (founder content + earned media in family law
publications and podcasts)
**Authenticity hook:** A concrete list of features or workflow gaps that the major platforms
do not address for family law specifically — ideally sourced from real practitioner complaints
(Reddit's r/LegalAdviceUK, The Law Society forums, family-law bar association discussions).
**Minimum viable version (2 weeks):** One long-form LinkedIn article: "What LexisNexis gets
right — and the three family-law workflows it still cannot solve." Specific, fair, and named.
Pitched to one family-law trade publication (e.g., Family Law Week, Resolution's member
newsletter — research as a week-1 action) for a guest column or quote.

---

### Concept C — "The Family Law Community Stack"
**Archetype:** Community-Build
**One-line thesis:** Build the Slack/Discord community for independent family lawyers that
does not exist yet — anchored to practice management, legal aid strategy, and case tool
sharing — and make the tool the trusted resource at the centre of it.
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (community node) + Tier 2 (organic LinkedIn to drive members
into the community)
**Authenticity hook:** The community is not a product demo channel; it is a genuine space for
family lawyers to share workflows, warn about judicial trends, and crowdsource precedent
approaches. The tool earns trust by being genuinely useful in the community before asking
for a trial.
**Minimum viable version (2 weeks):** Set up a Slack or WhatsApp group. Personally invite
20 family law practitioners from LinkedIn connections, The Law Society local groups, or
Resolution member lists. Post daily for 14 consecutive days before mentioning the product
at all. Measure whether conversations start without the founder prompting them.

---

### Concept D — "Free Template Library for Family Lawyers"
**Archetype:** Product-Led Growth / Demand Capture
**One-line thesis:** Publish a free, genuinely useful library of family-law document
templates, precedent guides, and workflow checklists that rank for the long-tail search
terms family lawyers actually search — and make the tool the natural next step.
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 2 (SEO content) + Tier 1 (founder-amplified distribution)
**Authenticity hook:** The templates are drafted or reviewed by a practising family lawyer,
not by a content team. The quality signal is real legal precision, not marketing language.
**Minimum viable version (2 weeks):** Publish three long-form, genuinely practitioner-grade
resources: (1) a consent order drafting checklist specific to the user's jurisdiction,
(2) a guide to MIAM exemptions (or equivalent jurisdiction-specific procedure), (3) a
financial disclosure timeline template. UTM-tag every download link. Track which terms
drive inbound.

---

### Concept E — "The Referral Flywheel via Resolution / The Law Society"
**Archetype:** Coalition Play + Referral Flywheel
**One-line thesis:** Partner with the professional associations family lawyers already trust
— Resolution (UK), the American Academy of Matrimonial Lawyers (US), or equivalent — for
a discounted or co-branded trial, and let peer endorsement do the work that ads cannot.
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (direct coalition partnership) + Tier 2 (association
newsletters and email lists)
**Authenticity hook:** A genuine endorsement from an association officer or committee
member who has actually used the tool — not a logo badge.
**Minimum viable version (2 weeks):** Identify and reach out to one regional or national
family-law association. Offer a free 3-month trial for their members in exchange for
feedback and, if warranted, a brief mention in their member newsletter. Research is the
week-1 action — success criteria is one confirmed conversation with an association contact
by end of week 2.

means you cannot be in a spending race at all — you are playing a different game. Broad paid

authentic practitioner voice, specific workflow relevance for family law, and the trust that
compounds through real community and peer referral.

## 3a. Message-Market-Fit Gate

Three yes/no questions. If you do not know the answer to one, treat it as "no."

**Q1 — Revenue/commitment signal:** Have 10 or more family lawyers paid for, signed up for,
or directly asked (without prompting) for the tool?

**Q2 — Language signal:** Can you name a specific pain that family lawyers articulate in their
own words — not inferred by you, but actually said, written, or posted?

**Q3 — Close signal:** If you asked 5 family lawyers in your target audience to commit today
(at your intended price / trial format), would at least 3 say yes?

**Assumed score for this plan: 2/3** (the brief implies the tool exists and is being used, but
gives no direct MMF confirmation signals).

**Verdict: Borderline.** The plan proceeds, but insert a **1–2 week validation cycle** as a
pre-campaign task before running the full Stage 4–5 playbook.

**Validation cycle (Week 0):**
- Conduct 5 structured discovery conversations with family lawyers who have not used the
  tool yet. Goal: extract one or two sentences in their own language that describe the specific
  pain the tool solves. These sentences become the campaign's copy.
- If Q1 is failing (fewer than 10 committed users), offer 10 family lawyers a free 30-day
  trial in exchange for a structured feedback session. Commitment to the feedback session
  is the MMF signal, not just tool access.
- Revisit the gate after Week 0. If 2/3 confirmed, proceed. If still 0–1/3, the bottleneck
  is the offer or the product, not distribution — do not run the campaign yet.

*The rest of this plan assumes the MMF gate passes after the Week 0 validation cycle.*

## 5. 70/30 (or 80/20) Allocation

**Allocation: 100% organic / 0% paid.** Categorical asymmetry rules out broad cold-paid
entirely. Paid amplification is conditionally available later under strict conditions:

- **When it becomes available:** after at least one LinkedIn post or SEO article has
  demonstrated genuine organic traction (saves, shares, thread-depth comments — not likes)
  in a 48h window, AND after the email list reaches 200+ confirmed family-law subscribers.
- **What it covers:** warm retargeting only — website visitors and email list. Never cold
  audiences.
- **Scale:** even when unlocked, paid should not exceed 10–15% of total campaign effort.
  Spending against LexisNexis's budget on cold audiences is not a game you can win. The
  organic trust stack is the moat.

Rationale: at 100× spend asymmetry, every pound of paid spend buys a fraction of the reach
the incumbent already owns. The CPM curve in legal-professional targeting is among the
highest on any platform (B2B professional audience, high intent, small total pool). The
math does not work. Organic is not the budget option; it is the strategically correct option.

## 6. Competitor Saturation Map

### LexisNexis (named in brief)

**(a) What they saturate:**
LexisNexis markets with enterprise production value: polished case-study videos, conference
sponsorships at major bar association events, multi-page spread ads in legal trade publications,
and sales teams targeting firm partners and IT procurement. Their messaging emphasises
comprehensiveness ("the most complete legal research database"), reliability ("trusted by the
world's top law firms"), and enterprise-grade integrations. The visual register is corporate
— suits, meeting rooms, logos of Magic Circle firms. Their pricing pages and sales motions
are built around multi-seat enterprise contracts, not solo practitioners.

**(b) The absence that becomes your signal:**
LexisNexis cannot authentically speak to the solo family lawyer sitting in a provincial
magistrates' court on legal aid rates, wondering whether a case is going to pay the rent.
Their saturation of the enterprise register is your cut-through. Every polished ad they run
is a reminder that they are not talking to that practitioner. Your absence from conference
sponsorships, your lack of a sales team, and your willingness to name the price on page one
are the message.

**(c) One-sentence positioning line:**
"Built for the family lawyer in the room with the client — not the procurement team in the
firm's IT department."

*Hold this sentence across every piece of content, every page of the site, and every
description of the tool in earned media. Every piece of content must reinforce it or be cut.*

### The other major legal-research incumbents (not named per industry-peer rule)

**(a) What they saturate:**
The other major legal-research platforms share LexisNexis's structural bias: built for
large-firm and academic users, priced accordingly, marketed through the same enterprise
channels. The category as a whole saturates the "comprehensive research database" positioning.

**(b) The absence that becomes your signal:**
No major platform currently owns the "family-law practitioner tool" positioning. The niche
is under-served in marketing terms even if some platforms technically cover family law. Your
specificity — consent orders, financial remedy, MIAM, CAFCASS, child arrangements — is
visible in a way that general research tools cannot match without losing their own
positioning.

**(c) Category-level positioning line:**
"The only tool designed around how family law is actually practised, not around how it is
researched in a law school library."

## 7. Three Alternative Campaign Shapes

Each shape includes a flagship piece of content structured as **Self / Us / Now** (Ganz).

---

### Shape 1 — Founder-Voice-First (Trust-Led)

**Description:** The founder (or a named practitioner spokesperson) posts on LinkedIn 3×/week
for 60 days — consistently, in their own voice, about the reality of family law practice
and the specific gaps the tool fills. No production budget, no agency copy. Raw, specific,
first-person.

**Flagship content (Self / Us / Now):**
- *Self:* "I spent three years watching family lawyers pay £3,000/seat for a platform built
  for M&A due diligence. Here is the specific workflow that does not exist in any of the major
  platforms and that I built for practitioners like us."
- *Us:* "Every family lawyer I know has the same story: you are in the consultation room,
  you need a precedent in 90 seconds, and the tool you have open is designed for someone
  else's problem."
- *Now:* "I have opened 20 free trial slots for the next 30 days. No sales call required.
  Try it on a real case and tell me where it breaks."

**Tradeoffs:**
- Requires a founder (or practitioner proxy) who can write candidly and consistently.
- Compounds slowly — expect 30–45 days before LinkedIn algorithm starts distributing beyond
  existing connections.
- Highest long-term ROI of the three shapes if sustained for 90+ days.
- **Fails if:** the founder stops posting after week 3 or switches to brand-voice copy.

---

### Shape 2 — SEO-Led Demand Capture (Search-First)

**Description:** Publish a free, practitioner-grade library of family-law templates, workflow
guides, and jurisdiction-specific procedure explainers. Target long-tail queries that family
lawyers actually search. The content earns organic traffic; the tool is the natural next step
for each piece of content.

**Flagship content (Self / Us / Now):**
- *Self:* "I built these templates because I could not find anything at this level of specificity
  for family law practitioners — everything I found was either too generic or locked behind a
  £3,000 subscription."
- *Us:* "If you work in family law, you have spent time searching for a consent order template
  that actually reflects current practice and does not require you to delete eight paragraphs
  about corporate litigation."
- *Now:* "Download the consent order checklist (free, no email required). If it saves you 20
  minutes on your next case, I would like to show you what the full tool does."

**Tradeoffs:**
- Does not require the founder to be a LinkedIn-native writer.
- Takes 60–90 days before Google starts ranking the content meaningfully.
- Once it ranks, it captures demand passively — the compounding is search-engine-based, not
  algorithm-based.
- **Fails if:** the content is not genuinely practitioner-grade. A template that a working
  family lawyer would not actually use is a trust liability, not an asset.

---

### Shape 3 — Coalition/Earned-Media-First (Borrowed Credibility)

**Description:** Secure one or two guest appearances, co-authored articles, or official
mentions in publications or podcasts that family lawyers already trust — and let those
endorsements do the credibility work before the founder's own audience exists.

**Flagship content (Self / Us / Now):**
- *Self:* "I want to be honest about what I built and who it is for. I have been pitching
  [association/publication] not because I need the exposure but because the people who read
  this have the specific problem I solved."
- *Us:* "Family law practitioners are routinely under-served by legal-tech. The tools we
  buy are priced for firms that do not practice family law and built for workflows that do
  not reflect our day."
- *Now:* "I have been given space in this newsletter to offer their readers a 60-day free
  trial. No pitch call. No enterprise procurement. Just try it."

**Target research (Week 1 action — targets TBD, research is the deliverable):**
The following are categories of targets to research; names must be confirmed by the founder
before outreach:
- Family law practitioner podcasts (search "family law podcast" + jurisdiction; produce a
  ranked list of 10 by episode frequency and listener engagement signals)
- Resolution member newsletter (UK)
- Family Law Week (UK)
- American Academy of Matrimonial Lawyers publications and webinar series (US)
- Law Society Gazette family law section (UK)

Success criteria for Week 1: a ranked list of 10 specific named targets with contact
channel and pitch hook, not a vague "pitch podcasts" note.

**Tradeoffs:**
- Fastest to credibility if a placement lands in week 3–4.
- Most dependent on outreach response rate — the founder does not control the outcome.
- Earned-media placements are the most powerful trust signal but the least predictable.
- **Fails if:** outreach is sent as a generic press release rather than a matched, specific
  pitch hook tied to the target's recent content.

## 9. Ad Copy + Boost Rules

**Categorical asymmetry applies: broad cold-paid creative is refused.**

Explanation: At 100× spend asymmetry against LexisNexis, any broad paid campaign — Meta
cold audiences, Google generic keywords, display — will produce a CPA that does not pencil
out against LexisNexis's share of voice in legal-professional targeting. Legal-professional
audiences are among the smallest and most expensive B2B segments on any ad platform. You
would be paying a premium CPM for an audience that LexisNexis has already reached dozens
of times before your ad appears.

**What is conditionally available (not before Week 4 at the earliest):**

When unlocked (after organic traction is confirmed and email list reaches 200+):
- **Warm retargeting only:** website visitors who viewed a template page and did not sign up,
  email subscribers who have not opened in 30 days, LinkedIn post engagers who did not click
  through. CPAs on warm retargeting run 2–3× better than cold in most B2B verticals.
- **24–48h organic traction gate:** do not boost any LinkedIn post until it has demonstrated
  genuine organic signal (saves, shares, thread-depth comments — not raw likes) within 48
  hours of posting. Only the posts that already worked organically are worth paying to extend.
- **Frequency cap:** maximum 5–6 impressions per person on any boosted post. Beyond 5–6
  impressions, conversion rates flatten and then go negative — you are paying to annoy people.
- **Creative refresh:** if boosting, refresh the creative every 2 weeks. Audience fatigue in
  small professional niches is faster than in consumer audiences.

**No brand-keyword bidding** without a geo-holdout lift test (Template 4 in the lift-test
plan below). The eBay field experiment (Blake, Nosko & Tadelis) showed weak or no measurable
incremental lift from brand-keyword bidding for established brands; for an emerging tool,
the same logic applies — the few people searching your brand name by name will find you
organically.

## 10. Lift-Test / Measurement Plan

**Template 5 — Organic-Source Attribution (zero-budget)**, with a clear decision rule.

The campaign starts with zero paid spend. No geo-holdout or audience-holdout is available.
Template 5 is the correct template for this phase.

**Hypothesis:** Among LinkedIn founder content, SEO template downloads, and earned-media
referrals, at least one channel will produce a measurably higher conversion rate per unique
visitor than the others — and that channel should receive a disproportionate share of the
monthly 6h effort going into Month 2.

**Setup:**
- UTM-tag every outbound link using the scheme: `utm_source=linkedin`, `utm_source=seo-template`,
  `utm_source=newsletter`, `utm_source=earned-<publication-slug>`, `utm_medium=organic`,
  `utm_campaign=family-law-practitioner-q2`.
- Install Plausible, PostHog, or Simple Analytics on the tool's landing/signup page (privacy-
  compliant, GDPR-safe for UK/EU audiences). Track: unique visitors by UTM source → trial
  signups.
- Run for 30 days minimum (Weeks 1–4 of the first-30-days list).

**Decision rule at Day 30:**
- Rank all channels by **conversion rate per unique visitor** (not absolute conversion volume).
- Top channel → double weekly effort in Month 2.
- Any channel at <20% of top channel's conversion rate → drop it or reduce to zero unless
  it serves a secondary function (SEO backlink, association credibility) the conversion
  metric cannot see.
- Overall conversion rate across all channels <0.5% → re-run the MMF gate before Month 2.
  The bottleneck is upstream — likely message-market fit or offer, not distribution.

**Caveat:** Template 5 is directional, not causal. You cannot prove any channel caused
its conversions without a randomised holdout. Treat the rankings as effort-allocation
guidance, not as proof of lift. When/if the tool accumulates a budget and the email list
grows past 500, graduate to Template 1 (geo-holdout) for real causal measurement.

**What I will not accept as a measurement plan:**
- "We will track ROAS in the Meta dashboard." Not applicable and attributed, not incremental.
- "We will measure follower growth." Confounded with everything; not actionable.
- "We will see if conversions go up." Without UTM attribution, you cannot tell which channel
  drove the change.

## 11. Anti-Vanity Metric Dashboard

### Track (these are signal)

| Metric | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Trial signups by UTM source (per unique visitor, not raw count) | This is the conversion rate per channel — the decision variable for effort allocation |
| LinkedIn: saves and shares per post | Saves and shares indicate content worth spreading; likes are cheap and lie |
| LinkedIn: comment-thread depth | Practitioners responding with their own specific experiences means the content is resonating, not just being skimmed |
| Template downloads by UTM source | Demand-capture signal from SEO; which specific topics drive inbound |
| Email list size and open rate (target >35%) | An owned, deliverable list; platform-independent |
| Earned-media responses and placements | Third-party endorsement is the highest-trust signal available without budget |
| Word-of-mouth referrals (tracked via "how did you hear about us?" in the trial signup flow) | If practitioners are telling each other, the flywheel has started |
| Trial-to-paid conversion rate | The ultimate MMF signal — the only metric that proves the offer works |

### Ignore (these are noise)

| Metric | Why to ignore it |
|---|---|
| LinkedIn follower count | Vanity; does not predict conversion or trial signups |
| Post impressions and reach | You are not trying to win a reach game against LexisNexis; you are trying to earn practitioner trust |
| Raw likes on any platform | Cheap, often bot-inflated, poorly correlated with conversion in B2B professional contexts |
| Website sessions (without UTM segmentation) | Aggregate traffic is meaningless; source-segmented conversion rate is the signal |
| Platform-reported ROAS (if any paid is ever run) | Attributed, not incremental. Always run a lift test before trusting this number |
| Social media engagement rate (without segmentation by content type and conversion follow-through) | A high engagement rate on a post that drives zero trials is not a success |
| Email open rate in isolation | Open rate without click-through and conversion downstream is a vanity metric; segment by what the openers actually do |

### Failure mode warning (B2B SaaS sector rider)

The B2B SaaS failure mode for this sector is **MQL theatre**: accumulating form-fill trial
signups that look like traction but never convert to paid. Track trial-to-paid conversion
from Day 1, not after 90 days. If the trial conversion rate is <10% after 30 trials, the
bottleneck is onboarding, pricing, or product fit — not distribution. Stop the campaign and
fix the conversion step before adding more top-of-funnel.

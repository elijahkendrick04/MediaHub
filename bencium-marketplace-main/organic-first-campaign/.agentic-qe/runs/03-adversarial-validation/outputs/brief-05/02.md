## 1. Campaign Ideas (5+ concepts across archetypes)

### Concept A — "The AI Hype Test"
**Archetype:** Counter-narrative  
**One-line thesis:** Senior engineers are drowning in AI hype; a course that teaches them how to separate signal from noise will earn trust by demonstrating it publicly before asking for money.  
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (founder LinkedIn, newsletter) + Tier 2 (earned media)  
**Authenticity hook:** The founder publishes a recurring "AI hype vs. real ROI" teardown — concrete examples from her OSS project and teaching experience — each piece ending with "this is what engineers actually need to know." The hype teardown is the top-of-funnel. The course is where the systematic version lives.  
**Minimum viable version:** Five LinkedIn posts in two weeks dissecting five overhyped AI claims with specific evidence from her engineering work. Measure saves and comments from engineers who say "this is exactly my situation." If five posts generate 3+ substantive replies from engineers in the target audience, the concept has signal.

---

### Concept B — "Build in Public, Teach in Public"
**Archetype:** Founder-story arc  
**One-line thesis:** The founder documents her own experience integrating AI tooling into real engineering work — the wins, the failures, the surprises — turning her learning process into the course's pre-marketing.  
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (LinkedIn, newsletter) + Tier 2 (TikTok/Reels for short-form clips)  
**Authenticity hook:** Every post references her actual OSS project (12k GitHub stars) as the test environment. Engineers who already trust the OSS work will transfer that trust to the course. No hypotheticals; every claim backed by a commit, a benchmark, or a real production decision.  
**Minimum viable version:** A newsletter issue titled "I spent 3 months forcing AI into my engineering workflow. Here is what actually worked." Measure unique opens among non-subscribers (forwarded opens) and LinkedIn reshares. If 10+ non-subscribers sign up after the issue goes out, the signal is there.

---

### Concept C — "The Waiting Room List"
**Archetype:** Referral flywheel  
**One-line thesis:** Because 9/15 pilot students came from the founder's network, the next cohort can be filled by systematically activating those 15 alumni as distributed recruiters — turning word of mouth into a structured referral loop before spending a euro on ads.  
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (alumni network, word of mouth, direct ask)  
**Authenticity hook:** The 15 pilot alumni are the proof. Real names, real outcomes, real referrals. The campaign starts by asking each alumnus for one honest public post and one direct recommendation to a colleague they think would benefit.  
**Minimum viable version:** Personal email to all 15 pilot students asking for (a) one LinkedIn post about their experience and (b) one direct referral to a colleague. Count how many do it unprompted. If 7+ respond, the flywheel is real. If fewer, investigate what friction is blocking the ask.

---

### Concept D — "The Open Curriculum"
**Archetype:** Product-led growth (course as distribution)  
**One-line thesis:** Publish the first module of the course as a free resource — not a watered-down teaser, but a complete, usable unit — so engineers experience the quality before paying, and share it with colleagues because it is genuinely useful.  
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 2 (GitHub, LinkedIn, newsletter, Hacker News) + Tier 1 (founder voice framing the release)  
**Authenticity hook:** The existing OSS project already has 12k GitHub stars — the audience trusts the founder's engineering judgment. A free curriculum release on GitHub fits the established pattern; it does not feel like a marketing move because it matches what she already does.  
**Minimum viable version:** Publish Module 1 as a GitHub repo or public Notion page. Post to Hacker News "Show HN" and the founder's LinkedIn. Track: stars/forks of the repo, newsletter signups from the landing page, and whether any engineers ask "how do I get the full course?"

---

### Concept E — "Earned-Media Certification Stunt"
**Archetype:** Earned-media stunt + coalition play  
**One-line thesis:** Partner with 3–5 well-known European engineering communities or tech newsletters to co-produce a "State of AI in Engineering" survey, then publish the results as a newsworthy data story — positioning the course as the response to what the data reveals.  
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 2 (press, partner newsletters, podcast) + Tier 1 (founder synthesis of the findings)  
**Authenticity hook:** The data is real, collected from the actual target audience. The founder is not claiming expertise — she is surfacing evidence from engineers themselves. The course announcement follows naturally: "You said this is your biggest gap. Here is where we teach it."  
**Minimum viable version:** Run a 10-question survey through the existing newsletter (4k subs, 38% open = ~1,500 readers responding). Publish results publicly before any course announcement. Measure whether engineering newsletters or podcasts pick up the findings as a story.

## 3. Spend Asymmetry Verdict

**Your asymmetry is mild to low-severe** (major course platforms have unlimited marketing budgets; cohort competitors in the niche spend €5–15k per cohort on ads vs. your €2,500/month total). This means you cannot win on reach spend, but you have a strong enough warm base and a distinctive asset profile (OSS credibility, strong newsletter engagement, testimonials) to build an organic engine that will outperform paid alternatives for your specific audience — sceptical senior engineers who are immune to ad-driven urgency.

The cohort-course competitors who spend €5–15k per cohort on ads are in mild asymmetry relative to your €2,500/month. The major platforms (Udemy, Coursera) are categorically outspending you, but they are not the direct threat — engineers who want a €20 Udemy course are not your buyers. Your real competition is the cohort-course niche, and there you are in mild asymmetry.

Practical implication: a 70/30 organic/paid split is the right posture. Paid spend should be small, targeted, and gated behind organic proof.

## 4. Channel Tier Stack

**Primary function: Trust compounding** (the bottleneck is reach-plus-trust, not pure demand capture; senior engineers who find the course need to trust the founder before they commit €1,200 and career time to a cohort)

**Sector rider applied: Rider 1 — Cohort-based education.** Key implications:
- The room is the product; market the room (peers, accountability, alumni network, direct feedback), not the curriculum.
- Community node (Slack or Discord for alumni and applicants) is the single highest-leverage channel.
- Referral flywheel through the attendee network compounds faster than any paid channel.
- Earned media anchored to teaching artifacts (published curriculum, case studies, student outcomes) fits this sector.
- **Failure mode to avoid:** selling information in a world where information is free. Never lead with "learn AI-assisted engineering" — lead with "join a cohort of senior engineers working through the same problems."

## 6. Competitor Saturation Map

*Specific competitor brand names not provided in the brief; staying at category level per anti-fabrication rules.*

### Competitor Category A — Major course platforms (unlimited marketing budget, €20–50 price point)

**(a) What they saturate:**  
Paid search for every "learn AI" and "machine learning for engineers" query. Google Display Network banners across tech content. LinkedIn carousel ads with "Master AI in 30 days" copy and stock-photo learners. Email remarketing sequences triggered by any browsing signal. Generic completions-count social proof ("14,000 students enrolled"). Heavy SEO investment on broad informational queries. The production quality is high, the specificity is zero, and the promise is frictionless (self-paced, cheap, no commitment).

**(b) The absence that becomes your signal:**  
Their entire funnel promises ease with no accountability. Your refusal to be self-paced, your small cohort size, your application process, and your price point are not disadvantages — they are the counter-positioning. When the platforms flood every channel with "learn at your own pace for €30," your deliberate friction (application, cohort format, €1,200, specific outcomes with real accountability) reads as the serious alternative for engineers who already know self-paced cheap courses do not change their practice.

**(c) One-sentence positioning line:**  
"This is not a course you complete — it is a cohort of senior engineers who will change how you work."

### Competitor Category B — Cohort courses in the niche (US-based teachers, $2k+, spending €5–15k per cohort on ads)

**(a) What they saturate:**  
Paid LinkedIn funnels with testimonial carousels and ROI-claim copy ("get promoted faster," "land a better role"). Affiliate link deals with engineering newsletters. Waitlist urgency sequences ("only 12 seats left"). Discount codes pushed through partner newsletters. Founder personal brands built on "growth hacking your engineering career" content rather than engineering craft content. US-centric examples and case studies.

**(b) The absence that becomes your signal:**  
Their marketing looks like marketing — aspirational, optimised, polished. Your open curriculum (actual teaching material published for free), your OSS project (12k GitHub stars from engineers who already trust your technical judgment), and your European context (same timezone, same regulatory environment, same engineering culture) are things they cannot manufacture. Your refusal to run affiliate deals or paid newsletter insertions is itself the message: "we grow because engineers recommend us to colleagues, not because we pay for placements."

**(c) One-sentence positioning line:**  
"Built by a European engineer for European engineers — no affiliate links, no paid placements, no hype."

## 7. Three Alternative Campaign Shapes

### Shape 1 — Community-First (Start with 100 real people)

**How it works:** Before any public announcement of Cohort 2, build a private community (Slack or Discord — recommend Slack given the professional audience) seeded with the 15 pilot alumni plus the founder's 50 closest LinkedIn connections in the target audience. The community is "AI-Assisted Engineering Practice" — not a course sales channel, but a place for senior engineers to share real experiences with AI tooling. The course is one of the things that happens inside the community, not the reason the community exists.

**Flagship content (Self / Us / Now):**
- *Self:* "I started this OSS project seven years ago because I was tired of AI tools that made junior engineers faster but didn't help senior engineers think better. I've spent the last year figuring out what actually changes."
- *Us:* "Every senior engineer I talk to has the same experience: the demos are impressive, the production results are embarrassing, and nobody wants to say so out loud because everyone else looks like they've got it figured out."
- *Now:* "I'm opening a private Slack for 100 senior engineers to talk honestly about what AI tooling does and does not do for them. No hype. No affiliate links. Link in my bio — 100 seats, application closes Friday."

**Tradeoffs:**
- Pro: community members become advocates for every future cohort; the network compounds; Rider 1's most recommended channel.
- Pro: high signal-to-noise for understanding what the next cohort needs.
- Con: slow to monetize; community must be live and healthy for 4–6 weeks before the first cohort announcement feels natural.
- Con: requires daily founder presence for the first 14 days (non-negotiable); at 8h/week total, this is the entire marketing budget for weeks 1–2.
- **Best fit for:** building the long-term asset that fills Cohorts 2 and 3 without paid spend.

### Shape 2 — Earned-Media-First (One newsworthy action drives press + organic amplification)

**How it works:** Publish the free open curriculum (Module 1 of the course) as a structured GitHub release with a companion article. Pitch it simultaneously to 3–5 European engineering newsletters and 2–3 engineering podcasts as "a European engineer published a free, complete AI-assisted engineering curriculum — here is why she built it and what makes it different from the hype." The curriculum is the stunt; the article is the narrative; the course is the call to action at the end.

**Flagship content (Self / Us / Now):**
- *Self:* "I published my first AI-assisted engineering curriculum today. Not a tutorial. Not a course teaser. The actual thing — Module 1 complete, free, on GitHub. I built it because the versions I could find were teaching syntax, not judgment."
- *Us:* "Senior engineers don't need to learn how to use Copilot. They need a framework for deciding when AI tooling improves their work and when it makes them slower. I've never seen a course that teaches that, so I wrote one."
- *Now:* "The full course runs as a 6-week cohort with 40 engineers. The next cohort opens in [Month]. Apply here — there are 40 seats and the curriculum tells you exactly what you are buying."

**Tradeoffs:**
- Pro: single newsworthy action creates a burst of cold reach without paid spend; best shape for reaching engineers who do not yet follow the founder.
- Pro: the free curriculum becomes a permanent SEO and word-of-mouth asset; every time someone discovers it, they discover the course.
- Con: earned-media pitching requires specific named targets and specific hooks; if the pitch does not match the newsletter's angle, it goes nowhere; see Week 1 action (research) in Section 8.
- Con: burst of attention is harder to convert than warm community trust; requires a clear, frictionless application path the day the coverage lands.
- **Best fit for:** Cohort 1 launch, where speed matters more than depth and the goal is proving the demand exists outside the existing network.

### Shape 3 — Search-Capture-First (Dominate long-tail queries where demand already exists)

**How it works:** Research the specific queries European senior engineers type when they are looking for AI-tooling guidance (not generic "learn AI" queries, but long-tail intent queries like "AI pair programming for experienced engineers," "how to evaluate AI code tools for senior engineers," "AI-assisted code review best practices"). Build 8–10 long-form posts on the newsletter/blog optimized for those queries, each ending with a clear application CTA. Supplement with LinkedIn posts that summarize each piece.

**Flagship content (Self / Us / Now):**
The flagship for this shape is a series lead piece, not a single post:
- *Self:* "I've been integrating AI tooling into my engineering workflow for 18 months. Here is my honest assessment — what works, what does not, and what took me embarrassingly long to figure out."
- *Us:* "The senior engineers I talk to all have the same experience: the junior-engineer use cases are obvious, and the senior-engineer use cases require judgment that no tutorial teaches."
- *Now:* "I'm publishing a complete series on this over the next six weeks — one post per week, free. Subscribe to get it when it drops, or apply for the cohort where we work through it together."

**Tradeoffs:**
- Pro: SEO compounds over time; a well-ranked long-tail post brings in cold organic traffic for months after publication.
- Pro: engineers who find the content via search already have intent; conversion rates are higher than social traffic.
- Con: slowest shape to produce results; first 3 months may show minimal traffic from new posts.
- Con: requires ops person to handle SEO setup, keyword research, and link structure — significant upfront investment.
- **Best fit for:** Cohort 3, after Cohort 1 and 2 have generated testimonials and case studies that become the most valuable SEO content.

**Recommended approach:** Run Shape 2 (Earned-Media-First) for Cohort 1 launch, Shape 1 (Community-First) to build the infrastructure for Cohorts 2 and 3, and add Shape 3 content (long-tail SEO) as an ops-person recurring task from Month 2 onwards.

## 9. Ad Copy + Boost Rules

**Paid applies here at mild asymmetry, with strict gates.**

### Creative direction

Voice: first person, founder's own voice. Not "the course offers" — "I built this because." Every paid creative should look like an organic post with a "sponsored" label, not like an ad.

Avoid: stock photography, polished layout with brand colours, testimonial carousels with headshots that look like they were art-directed. These signal "paid" to the target audience and trigger scepticism in senior engineers.

Use: phone screenshots of real GitHub activity, a photo from an actual teaching session with real students (with permission), a screenshot of a real student message.

### Copy example (LinkedIn Boost — open curriculum post)

Boost the organically performing post as-is. Do not rewrite it for paid. If the organic version earned 50+ saves, that is the content that should be boosted. Adding polish to it for the paid version strips the authenticity signal.

### Boost rules

1. **24–48h organic traction gate.** Only boost a post that has already earned: 50+ saves, 20+ substantive comments (not emoji-only), or measurable click-through to the application page. Likes do not count — they are inflated by network effects and do not predict conversion.
2. **Audience definition:** warm-first. Upload the newsletter list (4k subs) as a matched audience. Run lookalike expansion at 2× the warm list size maximum. No cold broad targeting.
3. **Budget per boost:** €200–400. Do not treat boosting as a continuous spend — run each boost for 7 days, then let it expire. Review before renewing.
4. **Frequency cap:** 5 impressions per person per 2-week window. Beyond 5 impressions, the campaign is paying to annoy engineers who have already decided not to apply.
5. **Creative refresh cadence:** every 2 weeks. Senior engineers develop ad fatigue faster than general consumer audiences; creative refresh is closer to 10–14 days than the old 4-week norm.
6. **Do not bid on the founder's own name keywords.** If she is running any Google Ads on her own name, require a geo-holdout test (Template 4 in the lift-test templates) before continuing that spend.

## 10. Lift-Test / Measurement Plan

**Template 5 — Organic-source attribution (zero-budget / directional measurement)**

This is the appropriate template because: (a) the paid budget is small relative to the minimum thresholds for a statistically powered geo-holdout experiment; (b) the primary campaign is organic; (c) the actionable question is "which organic channel is converting engineers into applicants," not "does paid spend produce incremental lift" (that question gets answered in Week 2 if the boost runs).

**Hypothesis:** LinkedIn organic content converts engineers into applicants at a higher rate per unique visitor than either the newsletter or the GitHub/Hacker News release, because the founder's LinkedIn posts are where cold-audience engineers first encounter the work — but the newsletter converts existing subscribers at a higher absolute rate despite lower unique visitor count.

**Measurement setup:**
- UTM scheme (to be live by end of Week 0): utm_source=linkedin / newsletter / github / hackernews / referral / podcast-[slug], utm_medium=organic, utm_campaign=cohort1
- Conversion event: application form submitted (not page view)
- Analytics: Plausible or PostHog with goal tracking on the application form submission URL
- Duration: 30 days from first LinkedIn post (Weeks 1–4)

**Per-channel conversion rate ranking at Day 30:**
- Divide application-form-submitted count by unique visitors from that UTM source
- Rank all channels highest to lowest conversion rate

**Decision rule:**
- Top channel: double weekly effort allocation in Cohort 2 campaign
- Any channel with conversion rate <20% of the top channel's rate: drop from active investment (keep the asset live, stop actively driving traffic to it) unless it serves a secondary credibility function (e.g., GitHub stars are brand equity even if they do not directly convert)
- Overall conversion rate across all sources <0.5%: the bottleneck is message or offer, not channel selection — re-run the MMF gate before Cohort 2

**Caveat:** Template 5 is directional only, not causal. Multi-touch attribution is underspecified — an engineer who applies via newsletter may have first encountered the founder on LinkedIn. Treat the rankings as a rough effort-allocation guide, not as proof of channel ROI. If the campaign generates any meaningful paid spend in Cohort 2, graduate to Template 1 (geo-holdout) or Template 2 (conversion-lift) for real incremental measurement.

**If the LinkedIn Boost runs in Week 2:**
Run a mini Template 4 (micro-lift): add "How did you hear about us?" to the application form. At the close of the application window, compare the share of applicants crediting the LinkedIn ad vs. organic LinkedIn. If the ad-attributed share is under 5% of total applicants, the boost did not materially contribute — do not scale it in Cohort 2.

## 11. Anti-Vanity Metric Dashboard

### Track these (they signal real momentum)

| Metric | Why it matters | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Application form submissions per cohort window | The only metric that translates to seats filled | Daily during application window |
| Conversion rate per UTM source (applications / unique visitors) | Tells you which channel is earning trust, not just clicks | Weekly (Template 5) |
| Newsletter open rate (target: maintain 35%+) | A dropping open rate signals list fatigue or message drift before it shows up in conversions | Per issue |
| Pilot alumni posts and referrals generated | Measures referral flywheel health; leading indicator for Cohort 2 | Weekly during Weeks 1–4 |
| Application-form "How did you hear?" responses | Ground-truth attribution; corrects UTM blind spots (e.g., dark social referrals) | Per cohort close |
| Post saves and thread-depth comments on LinkedIn | The organic traction gate signal — saves indicate "want to return to this," deep comment threads indicate genuine engagement | Per post, before any boost decision |
| Waitlist size for next cohort | Leading indicator of Cohort 2 demand before you start the campaign | Weekly from Week 4 onwards |
| Alumni NPS and referral rate (post-cohort) | The compounding asset; if alumni do not refer, the flywheel does not work | Per cohort completion |

### Ignore these (they lie or mislead)

| Metric | Why it misleads |
|---|---|
| LinkedIn impressions and follower count | Impressions measure reach, not trust or conversion. Follower count grows slowly and means nothing if open rate declines |
| Platform-reported ROAS from LinkedIn Boost | Attributed, not incremental. The application may have happened anyway from organic. Use the application-form self-report instead |
| Total newsletter subscribers (without open rate) | A list of 10k with 15% open rate is worse than 4k with 38% open rate. Size without engagement is vanity |
| "Engagement rate" without segmentation | An emoji like from a junior developer outside the target audience is not a signal. Count only substantive engagement (saves, multi-sentence comments, DM follow-ups) |
| GitHub stars on the open curriculum repo | Stars are credibility signals (useful for counter-positioning and SEO), not conversion signals. A star that never becomes an application is data, not a lead |
| Hacker News upvotes | Front-page HN traffic converts at 0.1–0.3% for paid products; the traffic is real, the conversion intent is low. Count it as brand equity, not pipeline |
| Podcast listener counts (if guesting) | Third-party audience size is irrelevant without conversion data. One podcast appearance to 500 highly relevant engineers outperforms one appearance to 50,000 general tech enthusiasts — measure UTM-tagged applications, not listener numbers |

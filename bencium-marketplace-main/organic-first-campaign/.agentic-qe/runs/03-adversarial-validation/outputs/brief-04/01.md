## 1. Campaign Ideas — 5 Concepts Across Archetypes

*Anti-fabrication note: the brief did not name specific competitors. All competitor references below stay at the category level ("established clean-beauty brands," "the category's incumbents") or use bracketed placeholders. No competitor proper nouns have been invented.*

---

### Concept A — "The Ingredient Audit"
**Archetype:** Counter-narrative  
**One-line thesis:** Every clean-beauty brand in the category makes the same claim — your counter-move is to audit the claims publicly, ingredient by ingredient, in the founder's real voice, making the category's deception the campaign's villain instead of the brands themselves.  
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (founder Instagram) + Tier 2 (email list, earned media on the audit methodology)  
**Authenticity hook:** The founder is the formulator. She can read an ingredient list in a way a sponsored influencer cannot. A screen-recorded INCI list walkthrough from the founder's phone — no studio, no script — is the format established brands cannot replicate.  
**Minimum viable version:** One Instagram carousel post: "I read the ingredient lists of the 5 best-selling 'clean' moisturizers in this country. Here's what I found." No brand names, no lawsuits — just the chemistry. Post Tuesday, watch saves and shares in 48h before deciding whether to expand.

---

### Concept B — "Skin Diary — 90 Days, One Routine"
**Archetype:** Founder-Story Arc  
**One-line thesis:** The founder has 18 months of skincare journey content — rather than more of the same, she turns the camera into a structured longitudinal story: one person, one routine, weekly photos, raw skin texture visible, no filters. The audience watches the experiment rather than receiving a pitch.  
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (founder personal Instagram, email series)  
**Authenticity hook:** Real skin, real timeline, visible pores, the two weeks her skin got worse before it got better. These are details a brand marketing team will not permit. That's exactly why they build trust.  
**Minimum viable version:** Announce the diary in an email to 420 subscribers. Post Week 1 photo with a paragraph about current skin state. Measure email click rate and Instagram saves. If either exceeds 5%, commit to the full arc.

---

### Concept C — "The 30-Sensitive-Skin Women Project"
**Archetype:** Community-Build  
**One-line thesis:** Recruit 30 women with sensitive skin from the existing follower base and email list for a structured product diary; they become the first micro-community, the first UGC engine, and the first referral cohort — turning customers into co-authors of the brand story.  
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (private WhatsApp group or Instagram broadcast channel as the community node, UGC as Tier 2)  
**Authenticity hook:** Real women, real names, real faces — not stock-photo testimonials. The community posts their own before/after, their own vocabulary about what sensitive skin feels like. The brand account reshares with permission. Established category brands cannot manufacture this without paying for it, which immediately reads as manufactured.  
**Minimum viable version:** Email existing 420 subscribers with an invitation to join a 4-week "sensitive skin diary" group. Goal: 30 participants. No incentive needed beyond early access and the founder's direct involvement. Launch when 20 people say yes.

---

### Concept D — "The No-Influencer Pledge"
**Archetype:** Earned-Media Stunt + Counter-Narrative  
**One-line thesis:** The category's signal problem is that everyone's founder story is now underwritten by a €2–5k-per-post influencer who has the same story about five other brands simultaneously. The counter-position is a public, specific, permanently held pledge: this brand does not and will not pay for any content. Every post about it is someone who bought it.  
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 2 (earned media — this has a news hook) + Tier 1 (founder explaining the pledge, in her own voice, from her kitchen)  
**Authenticity hook:** The pledge must be real and enforceable. The founder names what she is giving up (reach, paid reviews, gifted placements) and why. That specificity is the hook. Press in the clean-beauty, ethical-consumption, and small-business spaces will cover it if the pitch is sharp.  
**Minimum viable version:** Write a 600-word essay in the founder's voice explaining the pledge. Post on Instagram, send to email list. Research 10 journalists or newsletters in the clean-beauty/ethical-consumer space who have covered similar stories (this is Week 1 action). Pitch the essay as the hook. A single feature in a relevant newsletter produces the earned reach that €800/month in Instagram ads has not.

---

### Concept E — "Referral Before Launch" (Re-Engagement Flywheel)
**Archetype:** Referral Flywheel  
**One-line thesis:** The 420 email subscribers and 2,800 Instagram followers are a warm audience that has never been given a specific, peer-to-peer invitation to bring someone else in. A structured referral moment — not a discount programme, but a "bring a friend who has sensitive skin and I will reach out personally" — turns passive followers into active advocates.  
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (email to warm list, DMs to engaged followers)  
**Authenticity hook:** The founder writes the referral email personally, mentions actual customers by first name where she can ("I've been chatting with a few of you about X"), and frames the ask as community-building, not sales. No automated drip. One email. One DM to the 50 most engaged followers.  
**Minimum viable version:** One personal email to the 420-person list, asking for one forward. Track referral signups against a UTM-tagged landing page. If 15+ new subscribers arrive in 72 hours, the flywheel has a real gear ratio. If fewer, the offer or the ask needs reworking before scaling.

---

**Proceeding to Stages 3–5 with all five concepts in play, but weighting the full deliverable around the concept(s) most structurally suited to this campaign's constraints. Based on the brief's bottleneck (authenticity saturation, broken paid unit economics) and the sector rider (consumer brand / DTC), the plan leads with Concepts A and C as the twin engines, Concept D as the earned-media ignition event, and Concept E as the retention/referral layer. Concept B (Skin Diary) is absorbed into the founder-content cadence across all shapes in Stage 7.**

## 2. Selected Concept

**Primary engine:** Concept A (The Ingredient Audit — Counter-Narrative) as the lead content format, because the founder's formulator expertise is the one credential the category cannot manufacture.

**Secondary engine:** Concept C (The 30-Sensitive-Skin Women Project — Community-Build) as the retention and UGC layer, because repeat purchase economics and referral lift dominate the unit economics question at 9 months.

**Ignition event:** Concept D (The No-Influencer Pledge — Earned-Media Stunt) as the one-time earned-media move to generate borrowed reach in Month 1.

**Flywheel layer:** Concept E (Referral Before Launch) activated in Month 2 once community members have a product experience to share.

Concept B (Skin Diary) is folded into the ongoing founder-content cadence rather than treated as a standalone concept — the longitudinal skin documentation becomes the format for the founder's regular Instagram presence throughout.

## 3. Spend Asymmetry Verdict

Your asymmetry is **categorical** (competitors spend roughly 60× what you spend on paid social alone). This means paid channels are not a competition you can enter — the playbook shifts entirely to organic Tier 1–2, where production budget is irrelevant and where your formulator expertise and direct founder presence are assets the category leaders cannot manufacture without destroying their own polished positioning.

The current €800/month on Instagram ads is producing a €48 CPA against a €22 contribution margin per unit. That spend is currently destroying approximately €370/month in cash at breakeven — before accounting for time cost. Stopping it is not a sacrifice; it is a correction.

**Preconditions Check (6 factors):**

1. **Credible insider / formulator equivalent.** The founder formulates the products herself. She can explain ingredient chemistry in plain language in a way that a brand's marketing team or a paid influencer cannot verify. Score: **yes.**

2. **Accumulated grievance or unmet demand.** The brief names it precisely: "customers don't believe founder stories anymore because everyone has one." The category has saturated on authenticity claims to the point of credibility collapse. The grievance — being misled by clean-beauty marketing — is real and active. Score: **yes.**

3. **Consolidated challenger field.** The founder is a solo DTC brand in a field of many small solo brands. She is not the consolidated challenger. This is the most significant missing precondition: many small brands make the same counter-positioning claim, which dilutes it. Score: **partial — needs work.**

4. **Felt pain, not abstract pain.** Sensitive skin is experienced daily, personally, viscerally. A flare-up from a product that claimed to be clean is not an abstract grievance — it is a face-level, immediate, undeniable experience. Score: **yes.**

5. **Threshold-rewarding market / platform / system.** DTC personal care is not a winner-takes-all market, but Instagram's algorithm and word-of-mouth referral do reward consolidation: a brand with 10,000 engaged followers compounds differently than ten brands with 1,000 each. Modest compounding effect present. Score: **partial.**

6. **Incumbent overplaying a saturation hand.** The brief confirms it: category incumbents are running €2–5k-per-post influencer spend with science-backed, dermatologist-approved messaging — precisely the signals that have lost credibility with the target audience. Score: **yes.**

**Precondition score: 4/6.** Proceed with the playbook, but treat "consolidated challenger" and "threshold consolidation" as active sub-goals. The campaign must give the founder a specific, holdable position — not "we are also clean and honest" but one specific credential the category cannot contest. The formulator expertise is that position. Hold it without compromise across the entire 9-month window.

## 3a. Message-Market-Fit Gate

Three yes/no questions — evaluated against the brief:

**Q1 — Revenue/commitment signal.** Have ≥10 people in the target audience paid, signed up, or directly asked (without prompting) for what is being offered?

The brief states 420 email subscribers (built over 18 months) and 2,800 Instagram followers, with ongoing sales implied by the existence of a CPA figure (€48) and the math discussion. Sales have been occurring. Customers exist.
**Verdict: yes.**

**Q2 — Language signal.** Can the founder name a specific pain the audience articulates in their own words before being pitched?

The brief gives "sensitive skin" as the audience qualifier and names "clean/simple ingredient lists" as the demand driver. 18 months of posting about a personal skincare journey means the founder has had conversations with her audience and has received comments and DMs articulating the problem. The brief's framing of the bottleneck ("customers don't believe founder stories anymore") reflects audience language, not invented positioning.
**Verdict: yes — but the founder should explicitly catalogue 5–10 exact quotes from comments or DMs before drafting campaign copy. This is Week 1 action 1.**

**Q3 — Close signal.** If asked 5 people in the target audience to commit today (at the intended price / format / ask level), would at least 3 say yes?

The existing email list has a 28% open rate, which is meaningfully above category average for small brand lists (typically 18–22%). This indicates an engaged list. At a €35–45 price point for a product the subscriber has already expressed interest in, 3 of 5 committing is a conservative baseline given the engagement rate.
**Verdict: yes — directional confidence only; the Week 1 list segmentation exercise (see Section 8) will verify this against actual purchase history.**

**MMF score: 3/3 — confirmed.** The campaign is a distribution and trust problem, not a demand creation problem. The audience exists, the pain is real, the price point has been validated by existing sales. Proceed to Stage 4.

## 4. Channel Tier Stack

**Primary function:** Trust compounding. The bottleneck is not that people cannot find this brand — €800/month in Instagram ads has produced traffic. The bottleneck is that traffic does not convert at a margin-positive rate because the category has burned the "authentic founder story" trust signal for everyone. The function of this campaign is to build a specific, verifiable credibility layer that the category incumbents cannot match: formulator expertise, community co-authorship, and a public anti-influencer commitment.

**Sector rider applied:** Rider 4 (Consumer Brand / DTC). Key rider implications for this plan:

- UGC is a compounding asset class: every community member's post is a proof point for the next buyer. The 30-Sensitive-Skin Women project is the UGC engine.
- Repeat purchase economics dominate: the €15k/month revenue target at €67 AOV requires roughly 224 orders/month. Getting there via new-customer acquisition alone at a €48 CPA requires more budget than exists. Getting there via a mix of new customers and repeat buyers changes the math entirely. A 30-day repeat purchase rate of 20% means roughly 45 repeat orders without acquisition cost. That changes the acquisition math from "we need 224 new customers every month" to "we need ~180 new customers and keep 20% coming back."
- Rider warning carried forward to the anti-vanity dashboard: do not pay for influencer reach without organic proof. The brief already names this failure mode.

**Channel stack, prioritized:**

| Priority | Channel | Tier | Function | Weekly effort |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Founder personal Instagram (Reels + carousels + Stories) | Tier 1 | Trust compounding via formulator expertise | 3h/week |
| 2 | Email newsletter to 420-person list | Tier 1 | Trust compounding + direct sell to warm audience | 1.5h/week |
| 3 | Private community (WhatsApp Broadcast or Instagram Broadcast Channel) for the 30-person sensitive skin group | Tier 1 | Community-build + UGC generation | 1h/week (after seed sprint) |
| 4 | UGC resharing from community members + organic reviews | Tier 2 | Trust proof points for conversion-fence visitors | 0.5h/week |
| 5 | Earned media outreach (target: 10 relevant journalists / newsletters / podcasts — identified in Week 1) | Tier 2 | Borrowed audience reach via The No-Influencer Pledge story | 2h/week for 6 weeks, then 1h/week |
| 6 | Referral programme (activated Month 2) | Tier 1 | Word-of-mouth flywheel, CAC reduction | 0.5h/week sustained |
| 7 | Paid boost on proven organic winners only — 24–48h traction gate mandatory | Tier 3 | Amplification of confirmed resonant content only | Zero until a post clears the gate |

**Channels explicitly not in stack:**

- Broad cold Instagram ad campaigns: removed. The €48 CPA vs. €22 margin math is definitive.
- Paid influencer partnerships: removed. Category saturation means this channel now reads as manufactured — the opposite of the counter-positioning play.
- TikTok: the brief does not mention existing TikTok presence and the audience (women 30–50) indexes lower on TikTok than on Instagram. Not ruled out for Month 4+ if a post format emerges naturally, but not a priority for 10–12h/week capacity.

## 5. 70/30 (or 80/20) Allocation

At **categorical asymmetry (1:60 ratio)**, the allocation should be 95/5 organic/paid — and initially 100/0.

**Month 1–2:** 100% organic. The €800/month Instagram ad spend stops immediately. Those funds return to runway. No paid spend until a post demonstrates genuine organic traction AND a lift test design is ready.

**Month 3+:** If an Instagram Reel or carousel exceeds the 48h organic traction gate (saves >3% of reach, shares >1% of reach, genuine comments with skin questions — not generic engagement), a one-time boost of €150–200 to warm retargeting audiences only (past engagers, email list custom audience) is permitted. This is not a "paid strategy" — it is a single amplification event on a proven winner.

**Rationale:** At 1:60 asymmetry, paid social is not a channel this brand can compete in. The competitor's €50k/month buys algorithmic priority this brand cannot match. But that same spend is what has saturated the category and destroyed audience trust in paid content. Stopping the €800/month spend does not cost reach — it costs the illusion of reach that was producing a €26/unit loss. The organic-first playbook replaces the illusion with a real compound: 420 engaged email subscribers convert at a higher rate than cold Instagram impressions, and each community member who posts about the product reaches their own network without ad spend.

## 6. Competitor Saturation Map

*Anti-fabrication note: the brief described competitor behavior at the category level. No competitor proper nouns appear below. Descriptions are drawn directly from the brief's characterisation ("established clean-beauty brands," "influencer partnerships at €2–5k per post," "science-backed, dermatologist-approved, Instagram-aesthetic packaging") without inventing names.*

---

**Category competitors (established clean-beauty brands with paid social budgets of €50k+/month in region):**

**(a) What they saturate:**
- Paid Instagram and TikTok feeds, primarily Reels, with high-production-value content: clean studio backgrounds, dewy-skin models, ingredient close-ups with scientific overlay text.
- Mid-tier influencer partnerships (€2–5k per post) across accounts that have the same partnership structure with multiple brands simultaneously. The influencer's follower base receives three different "game-changing moisturiser" posts per month.
- Dermatologist co-branding: a quoted consultant (not an employee, not a formulator) provides the scientific authority signal. This credential is rented, not owned.
- Messaging uniformity: every brand in the category uses variants of the same claim set — "science-backed," "dermatologist-approved," "clean ingredients," "sensitive-skin tested." The vocabulary is indistinguishable across brands.

**(b) The absence that becomes the signal:**
Established clean-beauty brands cannot post unscripted content about how an ingredient actually behaves in a formula — because they do not have a formulator on camera, and because the legal and marketing review process prevents anything that could be interpreted as a specific efficacy claim without clinical backing. Their absence from the ingredient-chemistry conversation is structural. It is not a choice; it is a constraint.

The founder's presence in that gap — speaking as the person who actually made the formula, explaining why she chose X over Y, what she rejected and why — is a signal the category incumbents cannot manufacture without destroying their own polished positioning.

Additionally: every established brand is present in the paid layer and absent from community. They have audiences; they do not have members. Members talk to each other. Audience members watch and scroll. The difference compounds over 9 months.

**(c) One-sentence positioning line:**
**"Every ingredient in this product was chosen by the person whose name is on the jar — not approved by a consultant, not cleared by a marketing committee."**

This is the single sentence every piece of content must reinforce. When the founder posts an ingredient walkthrough, this is the sentence behind it. When a community member shares their experience, this is the sentence that contextualises it. When a journalist asks what makes this brand different, this is the sentence that opens the answer.

## 7. Three Alternative Campaign Shapes

Each shape uses the selected concept set (Ingredient Audit + Community-Build + No-Influencer Pledge) but routes differently based on where the founder chooses to spend her constraint (10–12h/week).

---

### Shape 1 — Formulator-First (Content-led, compounding)

**Premise:** The founder's Instagram becomes a weekly formulator's notebook. Every Tuesday: one ingredient, one decision, one specific detail about why it is or is not in the product. Bi-weekly: an ingredient audit of a category product (no brand names — just the INCI list, read aloud or walked through in a carousel, with specific chemistry). Monthly: one skin-diary update with unedited photos.

**Flagship content (Self / Us / Now):**
- *Self:* "I spent four years with recurring redness before I understood what was triggering it. None of the 'clean' products I tried told me what their emulsifiers were doing to my barrier. That's why I became a formulator — I needed to be able to read the thing I was putting on my face."
- *Us:* "If you have sensitive skin, you've been in this position: you bought the clean thing, and it made it worse, and nobody could tell you why. You weren't doing it wrong. The label wasn't giving you the information you needed."
- *Now:* "Every Tuesday I post one ingredient and exactly why it's in — or not in — my formulas. Follow if you want the version of clean beauty that comes with a chemistry explanation."

**Why it works:** The content is structurally impossible for established brands to replicate without dismantling their marketing structure. The format (unpolished, specific, first-person chemistry) is authenticity-maximal.

**Tradeoffs:** Slow. Month 1–3 will show modest engagement growth. Compounding becomes visible at Month 4–6 when the ingredient-audit archive starts appearing in search and saves accumulate. Requires the founder to be confident on camera talking chemistry — if she's not, this shape doesn't work.

**Time cost:** ~4.5h/week (3h content creation, 1h community response, 0.5h email).

---

### Shape 2 — Community-First (People-led, UGC engine)

**Premise:** The 30-Sensitive-Skin Women project launches first. The founder recruits 30 women from her existing list and followers, runs a 6-week structured skin diary with them (weekly check-in in a WhatsApp group or Instagram Broadcast Channel), and lets their documented experiences become the primary content. Founder posts are secondary — the members' posts are primary.

**Flagship content (Self / Us / Now):**
- *Self:* "I sent my products to 30 women with sensitive skin and asked them to document everything — including the bad days. I didn't review the content before they posted it. Here's what they shared."
- *Us:* "We've all been burned by before-and-after photos that don't look like our skin. These are real women I know by name, posting from their phones, with their real skin. That's the standard I want to be held to."
- *Now:* "Applications to join the next cohort open on [date]. 30 spots. If your skin is sensitive and you're willing to document it honestly, I want you in the group."

**Why it works:** UGC from 30 real women across 6 weeks generates content volume the founder cannot produce alone. Each member's post reaches their own network without ad spend. Community members who get results become the most credible referral engine possible.

**Tradeoffs:** Requires the 4-week seed sprint (daily founder presence in the group) described in Stage 5. If the founder cannot commit 14 consecutive daily posts in the community, this shape fails. Also requires logistics: product samples to 30 people, coordination, moderation.

**Time cost:** ~5h/week during seed sprint (Weeks 1–4), ~3h/week sustained.

---

### Shape 3 — Earned-Media-First (Borrowed audience, fast start)

**Premise:** The No-Influencer Pledge is the launch event. The founder writes a 600-word essay, posts it everywhere, and pitches it as an earned-media story to 10 targeted journalists, newsletters, and podcasters in the clean-beauty, ethical-consumer, and independent-brand spaces. The earned coverage provides a borrowed audience surge that cold Instagram ads cannot produce at this budget. Organic content and community-build follow the coverage.

**Flagship content (Self / Us / Now):**
The essay is the flagship:
- *Self:* "I launched this brand 18 months ago. I've been offered three influencer partnerships since then. I've turned them all down — not because I can't afford them, but because I think they're dishonest. Here's why."
- *Us:* "Every one of us who has bought a 'founder-made' product that turned out to be a white-label formula with a paid influencer's face on it has had the same experience: the story was the product, not the product."
- *Now:* "The pledge is permanent. If you see this brand's name in an influencer's post, and they're not explicitly labelling it as an unsolicited purchase they paid for themselves, something has changed. Hold us to it."

**Why it works:** Earned media provides reach that does not require ad spend and carries third-party credibility. One newsletter feature in a relevant publication with 20,000 subscribers is worth more in trust-building than €800/month in Instagram ads.

**Tradeoffs:** Earned media is not controllable. The story may not land with journalists. Week 1 requires research to identify specific targets; pitching takes 2–3 weeks; results take another 2–4 weeks. This is a slow ignition with a potentially large yield, not a steady weekly compounding system.

**Time cost:** 5h/week for Weeks 1–6 (research + writing + pitching), then drops to 1h/week for ongoing earned-media relationships.

---

**Recommendation for this brief:** Run Shape 1 and Shape 3 in parallel for the first 6 weeks — Shape 3 provides ignition (earned media for the pledge story), Shape 1 provides the compounding weekly cadence. Shape 2 (community) launches in Week 5 once the pledge story has produced some borrowed audience to recruit from. This is the full plan built out in Section 8.

## 8. First-30-Days Action List

**Capacity constraint:** 10–12h/week available. The action list below sums to approximately 9–10h/week in the first 4 weeks, respecting the ceiling and leaving 1–2h buffer for the unexpected (a press request, a community crisis, a formulation question that becomes a post).

**Cuts made:** TikTok content creation (would add 3–4h/week; removed — Instagram is the established primary platform for this audience and this founder), LinkedIn presence (irrelevant for B2C personal care at this stage), any paid ad management (€800/month campaign paused in Week 1).

---

### Week 1 — Foundation and ignition preparation

**Action 1.1 — Pause Instagram ads (Day 1)**
Owner: founder. Effort: 15 minutes. Log into Meta Ads Manager, pause all active campaigns. Keep the account open — the custom audience data (past website visitors, email list upload) will be used for potential future warm retargeting.
Success signal: €800/month freed. CPA experiment concluded.

**Action 1.2 — Audience language audit (Days 1–3)**
Owner: founder. Effort: 2h. Review the last 90 days of Instagram comments and DMs. Pull 10–15 exact quotes from people describing their sensitive-skin experience or their frustration with the clean-beauty category. Write them down verbatim. These become the "Us" language for all campaign copy.
Success signal: 10+ specific quotes documented in a notes file.

**Action 1.3 — Write The No-Influencer Pledge essay (Days 2–5)**
Owner: founder. Effort: 3h. 600–800 words, first person, in the founder's natural register. Use the Self/Us/Now structure. No corporate language. Specific about what partnerships were declined and why (without naming brands). End with the specific, permanent, verifiable commitment.
Success signal: essay written, read aloud once to check register, shared with one trusted person for a gut-check read.

**Action 1.4 — Earned-media target research (Days 3–7)**
Owner: founder. Effort: 3h. Identify 10 specific publications, newsletters, or podcasts in the clean-beauty, ethical-consumer, or independent-brand space that have covered similar "founder vs. industry" stories in the past 12 months. For each target: record the publication name, the journalist or editor's name (not invented — research actual bylines), their contact channel, and one sentence about a recent piece that the pledge essay would be a natural follow-on to.

*Anti-fabrication constraint: specific target names cannot be listed here because the brief did not name them and this skill cannot invent proper nouns. This research step IS the Week 1 action. The output of the research — a ranked list of 10 real targets with contact details and pitch hooks matched to their recent content — is the Week 1 deliverable.*

Success signal: a ranked list of 10 targets with names, contact channel, and one-sentence matched pitch hook. Due by end of Week 1.

**Action 1.5 — Email the list (Day 7)**
Owner: founder. Effort: 1h. Send the pledge essay to the 420-person email list. Subject line in the founder's voice, not a marketing subject line. Add one specific ask at the end: "If this resonates, forward it to one person you know who has given up on clean beauty."
Success signal: >28% open rate (matches existing baseline), >5% click rate to the Instagram post version.

**Total Week 1 effort: ~9.25h**

---

### Week 2 — First ingredient content + community recruitment open

**Action 2.1 — Post Ingredient Audit #1 on Instagram (Day 8)**
Owner: founder. Effort: 2.5h (research, writing, filming or carousel creation, posting). Format: either a 3–5 slide carousel or a 60–90 second Reel. Topic: one ingredient common in "clean" products that has a documented sensitising history in some skin types. Explain the chemistry in plain language. No brand names. No scare-mongering — just the mechanism and why she made a different choice.
Success signal: monitor for 48h. If saves exceed 3% of reach and shares exceed 1% of reach — this post passes the organic traction gate. Flag it. It becomes the first candidate for a small warm-audience boost in Month 3.

**Action 2.2 — Pitch earned-media targets (Days 8–10)**
Owner: founder. Effort: 2h. Send personalised pitches to the top 5 targets from the Week 1 research list. Each pitch: 3 sentences maximum. Opening sentence references their recent relevant piece specifically. Second sentence is the hook for the pledge story. Third sentence offers the essay as a contribution or interview.
*Outreach channel and day: Tuesday morning via email for newsletter/publication targets; Thursday morning via LinkedIn or Instagram DM for podcast hosts. Match the channel the target actually uses.*
Success signal: at least 1 reply within 10 days.

**Action 2.3 — Community recruitment email + Instagram Story (Day 9)**
Owner: founder. Effort: 1h. Send a specific invitation to the email list and post an Instagram Story: "I'm looking for 30 women with sensitive skin to document a 6-week product diary with me. You'll get the product. I'll be in the group every day. Your skin story — including the bad days — is what I need."
Link to a simple Google Form or Typeform: name, skin type, what they've tried, why they're frustrated.
Success signal: 20+ applications by end of Week 2.

**Action 2.4 — Skin Diary — Week 2 founder update**
Owner: founder. Effort: 1h. Post one phone-camera photo of founder's skin current state. Two paragraphs: one about what's working, one about what isn't. No filters. Tag it as part of an ongoing series.
Success signal: at least 15 comments from people describing a similar experience.

**Total Week 2 effort: ~6.5h** (pitched outreach is batched with Week 1 research time; some overlap)

---

### Week 3 — Community seed sprint begins + ingredient #2

**Action 3.1 — Launch the community group (Day 15)**
Owner: founder. Effort: 1h setup, then 30 min/day for 14 consecutive days. Set up WhatsApp Broadcast Channel or Instagram Broadcast Channel. Add the first 20–30 accepted participants. On Day 15: post the group rules, the format (weekly skin check-in, photo optional, honest reactions only), and the founder's Day 1 post about her own skin this week.
Non-negotiable: 14 consecutive daily founder posts in the community. This is not optional. Without the founder's daily presence, the community does not reach escape velocity.
Success signal: >50% of members post at least once in their first 7 days.

**Action 3.2 — Ingredient Audit #2 (Day 16)**
Owner: founder. Effort: 2.5h. Same format as Audit #1. Topic should respond to an actual question from comments or DMs — "Someone asked me last week about X, so this week I'm going into it."
This specificity (responding to the audience's actual questions) is structurally impossible for category brands to replicate at speed.

## 9. Ad Copy + Boost Rules

**Current state:** all paid Instagram campaigns paused in Week 1. Zero ad spend in Months 1–2.

**Boost rules for Month 3+ (if an organic winner is confirmed):**

**The 24–48h organic traction gate is mandatory.** A post is eligible for boosting only when, in its first 24–48 hours of organic posting, it demonstrates:
- Saves: >3% of total organic reach
- Shares: >1% of total organic reach
- Comments: substantive (skin questions, personal experiences shared — not emoji responses)

Likes do not count toward the gate. Likes are cheap, frequently bot-inflated, and do not predict conversion.

**If a post clears the gate:**

Audience definition for any boost:
1. Warm retargeting first: Instagram engagers (past 90 days) + email list custom audience uploaded to Meta.
2. Lookalike audience second: 1% lookalike of the email list (420 subscribers). This is a small seed — the lookalike will have wide confidence intervals, so spend here should be small and measured.
3. Cold audiences: not permitted. At 1:60 asymmetry, cold audience paid social cannot win on this budget.

**Budget per boost:** €150–200 maximum for a 5-day run. Frequency cap: 3 impressions per user maximum. After 5 days, let it end. Creative refresh for any new boost: new creative — do not re-boost the same post.

**Creative direction (drawn from the authenticity playbook):**

- Format: the post as-is. Do not re-produce paid creative from scratch — the organic post's unpolished quality IS the authenticity signal. Boosting a re-edited, polished version destroys the asset.
- Voice: first person, founder speaking as herself. No "we." No brand-speak.
- Specificity beats production value: a post that shows an ingredient label with a handwritten annotation outperforms a studio-shot ingredient photo with overlay text.
- Do not add a sales CTA to a content post before boosting. If the content worked without a CTA, the boost should amplify the content — let the conversion happen through the profile link or the follow action, not a "shop now" overlay.

**Brand-keyword bidding:** The brand is new enough that this is unlikely to be active. If Google Ads brand-keyword campaigns are running, apply Template 4 (brand-keyword holdout) before continuing. The eBay field experiment evidence is clear: brand-keyword ad spend on a brand with strong organic SEO produces minimal incremental lift at full cannibalization cost.

## 10. Lift-Test / Measurement Plan

**Template 5 — Organic-source attribution (zero-budget), mandatory from Day 1.**

*Rationale: at categorical asymmetry with zero current paid budget and a broken paid-unit-economics history, the immediate priority is not measuring paid lift — it is measuring which organic channels convert real customers. Template 5 is the correct starting template. If Month 3 paid boosting begins, a micro-lift supplement (Template 4, micro-version) is added.*

**Hypothesis:** Organic content (Instagram ingredient-audit format and email newsletter) drives higher conversion rates per unique visitor than passive brand-awareness content. Specifically, posts where the founder demonstrates formulator expertise will produce higher save rates and downstream email-list conversions than generic skincare advice posts.

**Setup:**

UTM scheme — tag every link published:
- Email newsletter links: `utm_source=email&utm_medium=newsletter&utm_campaign=ingredient-audit`
- Instagram bio link (changes to match current campaign push): update with `utm_source=instagram&utm_medium=bio-link&utm_campaign=<week-topic>`
- Referral links (from Week 4 referral invite): `utm_source=referral&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=referral-fw1`
- Community member reshares (where trackable): `utm_source=ugc&utm_medium=instagram-story&utm_campaign=community-diary`

Conversion events to track (in order of value):
1. Email list signup (primary acquisition metric)
2. Product purchase (revenue metric — pull from Shopify or equivalent)
3. Community group join (retention metric)

Analytics tool: Plausible, PostHog, or native Shopify analytics — whichever is already installed. Add UTM parsing if not already active.

**Duration:** 30 days minimum per measurement cycle. First review: end of Month 1 (Week 4 action 4.3).

**Ranking metric:** conversion rate per unique visitor, by channel (not absolute conversion volume).

**Decision rule:**
- Top channel → double weekly effort in Month 2.
- Any channel at <20% of top channel's conversion rate → drop the channel or pause it for 30 days and test a different format.
- Middle band → maintain effort, retest after next format change.
- If overall conversion rate across all channels falls below 0.5% in Month 2: the bottleneck has shifted upstream — likely the offer (price point, trial mechanic, or the product page itself). Revisit the MMF gate signals before adding more distribution effort.

**Month 3 lift test supplement (if boost spend begins):**

Apply Template 4 (micro-lift), not Template 1 (geo-holdout) — budget is too small for geo-holdout to be statistically powered.

Design: add "How did you first hear about us?" to the post-purchase confirmation screen. Options: saw an Instagram post / Instagram Story / forwarded email / friend told me / searched online / saw an ad. Run for 14 days of any boost period.

If "saw an ad" accounts for <10% of purchase credit: the boost is cannibalizing organic discovery. Stop.
If "saw an ad" accounts for >25%: the boost is producing incremental awareness. Continue with the same creative, same audience, same cap.

**Explicit caveat (Template 5 directional nature):** This measurement approach is directional only. Without a randomised holdout, we cannot prove any channel caused its conversions — someone who clicked the email link may have first seen the post on Instagram. Treat the rankings as rough guides to effort allocation, not as proof of lift. Graduate to Template 1 (geo-holdout) if any budget becomes available for a formal experiment.

## 11. Anti-Vanity Metric Dashboard

### Track these — they predict real outcomes

| Metric | Why it matters | How to measure |
|---|---|---|
| Email list signups per week | Platform-independent audience growth; these people gave you a direct line | Email platform analytics, UTM-tagged links |
| Email open rate (baseline: 28%) | If this drops, the content has lost relevance to the list | Email platform analytics |
| Instagram saves per post | Saves indicate "I want to come back to this" — the strongest Instagram signal for compounding reach | Instagram Insights |
| Instagram shares per post | Shares indicate "I want someone else to see this" — the word-of-mouth signal | Instagram Insights |
| 90-day repeat purchase rate | The unit economics question: are people coming back? | Shopify / order analytics, cohort view |
| Community active participation rate | Are members talking to each other, not just receiving? | Group analytics or manual count |
| Contribution margin per acquisition channel | Post-COGS, post-shipping: which channel produces customers who actually return the margin? | Orders by UTM source × average margin |
| Referral rate (organic) | What % of new customers arrive via word of mouth? | "How did you hear about us?" field, UTM-tagged referral links |
| Earned media placements | Coverage secured, newsletter features, podcast appearances | Manual tracking log |

### Ignore these — they measure activity, not outcomes

| Metric | Why it lies |
|---|---|
| Instagram follower count | Follower growth does not predict revenue; a 5,000-follower brand with 28% email open rates outperforms a 50,000-follower brand with 1% email engagement |
| Post likes | Cheap, frequently bot-inflated, do not predict saves, shares, or purchase |
| Impressions / reach | Reach above the first 5–6 exposures does not persuade — it annoys. More impressions from unengaged audiences is not progress |
| Platform-reported ROAS (if any paid spend resumes) | Attributed, not incremental. See Section 10. Platform ROAS figures are graded on correlated conversions, not caused ones |
| Story views | Views without profile taps, link clicks, or DMs are passive exposure, not engagement |
| Engagement rate (platform-reported, unweighted) | Raw engagement rate without segmentation mixes saves and likes — exactly the metrics with different predictive power |
| CPM / CPC (if paid resumes) | Cost metrics measure spending efficiency, not outcome efficiency. A low CPM reaching people who never buy is worse than a high CPM reaching people who do |
| Community member count | Member count means nothing without active participation. A 500-member broadcast channel with 3% participation is weaker than a 30-member active group |

## 1. Campaign Ideas — 5 Concepts Across Archetypes

### Concept A — "The Neighbor Running" (Founder-Story Arc)
**One-line thesis:** The candidate's two years on city council — visible, accessible, unpolished — is the sharpest contrast available against an incumbent who communicates through billboards and sponsored supplements. The Self/Us/Now story is already lived; it just needs a vehicle.

**Archetype:** Founder-story arc

**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 — candidate's personal Facebook and TikTok, Discord, in-person touchpoints

**Authenticity hook:** Specific, nameable moments from the 60 neighborhood meetings already completed. Real constituents, real complaints, real follow-through documented publicly. Not a position paper — footage of the candidate in a kitchen or a small-business backroom, listening.

**Minimum viable version:** Candidate posts one 90-second, phone-filmed "what I heard this week" video to personal Facebook and TikTok every week for 4 weeks. No production. Specific name of neighborhood, specific issue raised, specific what-I'll-do. Track saves and shares (not likes) as the traction signal.

---

### Concept B — "The Information Gap" (Counter-Narrative)
**One-line thesis:** The [3-term incumbent]'s saturation of state TV, sponsored supplements, and billboard space has produced a city full of people who hear one version of the story. The campaign's job is not to out-shout that machine — it is to quietly fill the information gap the machine cannot reach: independent Facebook groups, Discord, door-to-door, WhatsApp chains.

**Archetype:** Counter-narrative

**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 — independent Facebook groups, volunteer-distributed print, WhatsApp dark social; Tier 2 — earned media in non-captured local press and regional podcast/YouTube equivalents

**Authenticity hook:** Concrete fact-checks of specific incumbent claims — "The billboard says infrastructure X was delivered. Here is the city budget line for infrastructure X." Verifiable, specific, shareable.

**Minimum viable version:** One two-page, print-ready "reality check" document distributed via 5 Facebook groups and hand-delivered at one neighborhood meeting. Track inbound requests for more copies.

---

### Concept C — "Island Network" (Community-Build + Mobilization)
**One-line thesis:** Fifty volunteers organized into neighborhood-level autonomous cells — each with a local name, a local recruiter, and a local task — compound faster than any centralized campaign because every cell recruits the next cell from its own social network. The incumbent cannot buy this structure; it requires trust the incumbent has spent down.

**Archetype:** Community-build → Movement mobilization

**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 — neighborhood WhatsApp groups, local Facebook Groups, in-person chapter meetings; Tier 2 — Discord coordination hub

**Authenticity hook:** Named local people running named local groups. The candidate is visible but not the bottleneck. Each island coordinator's credibility is their own — employer, neighbor, parent of a school friend. The opponent's paid network cannot replicate this provenance.

**Minimum viable version:** Convert 10 of the 30 existing volunteers into named "neighborhood coordinators." Each coordinator identifies 5 people in their personal network to contact directly. No campaign materials required for week 1 — just personal messages from known people.

---

### Concept D — "Open Budget" (Earned-Media Stunt + Cause-Led)
**One-line thesis:** Release a full, itemized, public account of every euro the campaign spends — updated weekly — and make the contrast with the incumbent's opaque budget a standing story. Journalists in non-captured outlets cover it because it is newsworthy; supporters share it because it is proof of values.

**Archetype:** Earned-media stunt + cause-led

**Primary channel tier:** Tier 2 (earned media, shares); Tier 1 (candidate voice documenting it)

**Authenticity hook:** The real numbers, posted publicly, with no PR layer. Not "we're a grassroots campaign" — the actual spreadsheet. The credibility comes from verifiability.

**Minimum viable version:** Post the full campaign budget-to-date on the candidate's Facebook on week 1 with a one-paragraph explanation of why. Pitch the local non-captured journalists on the angle the same day.

---

### Concept E — "Returning Voices" (Referral Flywheel + Coalition Play)
**One-line thesis:** The young professionals who left the city and returned, and those who stayed despite the pull to leave, share a specific grievance the incumbent cannot address — the city's failure to create the conditions that make staying worthwhile. Organizing this segment as recruiters, not just voters, turns their existing networks into a turnout machine.

**Archetype:** Referral flywheel + coalition play

**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 — Discord (already 800 members), personal network asks, WhatsApp chains; Tier 2 — local professional networks, alumni networks of nearby universities

**Authenticity hook:** Specific, named young professionals willing to publicly say why they returned or stayed and what they need to see change. Not campaign messaging — their own words, their own faces, their own platforms.

**Minimum viable version:** Ask 5 of the most active Discord members to each record a 60-second personal video on their own phone explaining what brought them back (or what keeps them) and what they want from the next mayor. Share in the Discord. Track shares outside the Discord as the traction signal.

## 2. Selected Concept

The brief does not specify a selection — this is a full deliverable. Based on the brief's existing assets and the categorical asymmetry assessment below, the recommended primary concept is **Concept C — "Island Network"** (community-build + mobilization) as the organizational spine, with **Concept A — "The Neighbor Running"** (founder-story arc) as the candidate's personal content layer running in parallel. These two are mutually reinforcing, not competing: Concept A produces content the network distributes; Concept C builds the network that distributes it.

Concepts B and D are secondary activations, not separate campaigns. "The Information Gap" counter-narrative function is embedded in the Island Network's local print and group messaging. "Open Budget" is a single earned-media action in week 1, not a standalone campaign shape.

Concept E ("Returning Voices") is the targeted young-professional activation within the Island Network model — a sub-campaign for the Discord segment.

The analysis below runs Concepts A + C as the selected combined concept.

## 3. Spend Asymmetry Verdict

**Your asymmetry is categorical (1:50+ on direct spend; non-comparable when state media is included).** This means you cannot be in a spending race at any level — the entire campaign must be built on assets the incumbent cannot buy: volunteer networks, personal narrative, community nodes, earned media in non-captured outlets, and door-to-door human contact. Broad cold-paid media is refused for this campaign.

### Preconditions Check (scored against the 6 factors)

1. **Credible insider / founder-story equivalent.** The candidate has two years of visible city-council work, 60 completed neighborhood meetings, and an existing 12k-follower base built without paid spend. This is a real record, not a managed persona. **Score: YES.**

2. **Accumulated grievance or unmet demand.** A 3-term incumbent in a partially media-captured environment typically accumulates grievance through service failures, cronyism perception, or infrastructure promises unrealized. The brief signals this with the incumbent's "stability and continuity" messaging — which is the message of a campaign defending against change pressure. Grievance exists; it needs a named vehicle. **Score: YES (confirm with the candidate which specific grievances are most felt — see first-30-days action item).**

3. **Consolidated challenger field.** The brief describes one candidate against one incumbent. Fragmented opposition is not described. If other candidates are running, this precondition requires confirmation. **Score: YES — conditional on no significant vote-splitting from third candidates.**

4. **Felt pain, not abstract pain.** Small business owners experiencing permit delays, public-sector workers experiencing wage stagnation, young professionals experiencing housing and economic conditions that push them to leave — these are direct, experienced costs, not abstract policy positions. The 60 neighborhood meetings suggest the candidate already has language for this. **Score: YES.**

5. **Threshold-rewarding system.** A mayoral election is a plurality or majority system — one seat, winner-take-all. Organic mobilization that crosses the threshold wins everything. The consolidation dynamic rewards the campaign that turns out more votes in more precincts, not the one with the highest impression count. **Score: YES.**

6. **Incumbent overplaying a saturation hand.** Billboard saturation, sponsored newspaper supplements, state-TV coverage, Facebook ad flooding — the brief describes a classic saturation play. In a 180,000-person city where the incumbent has been in office for 3 terms, the marginal persuasion value of the 1,000th billboard is negative. Every additional piece of polished incumbency messaging is an opportunity for contrast. **Score: YES.**

**Preconditions score: 5–6 / 6 (conditional on #3).** Run the organic-first playbook at full scale. Proceed to Stage 4.

## 3a. Message-Market-Fit Gate

**MMF assessment for a mayoral campaign:**

1. **Revenue/commitment signal.** Have ≥10 people in the target audience directly committed to supporting this candidacy without prompting? The 30 active volunteers and 800-member Discord suggest yes — these are people who joined, attended, or committed without being paid to. **Score: YES.**

2. **Language signal.** Can the campaign name a specific pain the audience articulates in their own words, before the pitch? The 60 neighborhood meetings are a corpus of exactly this. The candidate has heard real constituents name real problems. The brief does not reproduce the language, but the meetings exist. **Score: YES — on the condition that this language is documented and used as campaign copy, not paraphrased into policy-speak.**

3. **Close signal.** If the candidate asked 5 voters in the target audience to commit to voting for them today, would at least 3 say yes? With 12k Facebook followers and 30 active volunteers, the answer is almost certainly yes within the existing base. The question is whether it's yes beyond the base — which is the campaign's job to expand. Within current supporters: **YES.**

**MMF verdict: 3/3 — confirmed.** The campaign is a distribution and mobilization problem, not an MMF problem. The candidate has an existing base, a real record, and constituent language from field work. The bottleneck is reach and turnout organization beyond the existing base. Proceed to Stage 4.

## 4. Channel Tier Stack

**Primary function: Trust compounding + Mobilization.**

The bottleneck is not demand capture (voters know who the incumbent is and they know this candidate exists within the existing base). The bottleneck is: extending trust past the existing 12k-follower base to the larger registered voter population, and converting trust into organized turnout in every precinct. This is a trust-compounding + mobilization function, not a paid amplification function.

**Sector rider applied: Rider 5 — Political/Movement/Civic.**

Key adjustments from the rider:
- Ground game math: volunteer door-knock is ~45× more persuasive than a paid impression in the same household. Turnout in the last 72 hours is a function of organized human contact, not ad spend.
- Primary channel fit: Tier 1 (volunteer networks, chapter/precinct structure, candidate personal presence on Facebook and TikTok from unscripted locations) and Tier 2 (counter-media in information-dark zones — local non-captured press, regional podcast/YouTube, Facebook Groups as the surviving organic surface on Meta).
- Archetype defaults confirmed: founder-story arc (candidate's lived experience, Ganz Self/Us/Now), counter-narrative (name the incumbent's saturation, refuse to match it), community-build/mobilization, coalition play.
- Common failure mode to watch: fragmented opposition. If other candidates are running, the campaign's biggest risk is vote-splitting. Coalition or non-competition agreements with other challengers should be explored in the first 30 days. (Named explicitly in the anti-vanity dashboard.)

**Categorical asymmetry rule applied:** Tier 1 and Tier 2 only. No broad cold-paid. The €25,000 budget is reserved for: print materials (volunteer-distributed newspaper/flyer), event costs, digital tools, and Tier 3 warm retargeting of the existing email/Facebook audience as a small late-campaign activation (see allocation below).

## 5. 70/30 Allocation

**Allocation: 92% organic (Tier 1 + Tier 2) / 8% paid (Tier 3 warm retargeting only, last 6 weeks).**

At categorical asymmetry, the skill's rule is Tier 1 and Tier 2 only, with paid refused for broad cold audiences. The 8% paid allocation is a narrow carve-out for warm retargeting of the existing Facebook follower base and Discord members in the final 6 weeks — this is not cold paid advertising, it is reminding people who already support the candidate to actually vote and to bring one person with them.

**In budget terms:** €25,000 total.
- ~€3,500 — warm retargeting (final 6 weeks, Tier 3, existing audience only)
- ~€8,000 — volunteer-distributed print newspaper (design, print, distribution logistics)
- ~€6,000 — candidate travel, event hosting, field operation costs
- ~€4,000 — digital tools, UTM tracking, analytics, campaign website
- ~€2,500 — earned-media activation (press kits, event costs tied to newsworthy actions)
- ~€1,000 — reserve

**Rationale:** At 1:50+ asymmetry, every euro spent on cold paid advertising is an euro that does not fund a volunteer's canvassing materials, a neighborhood meeting, or a printed newspaper for a media-dark zone. The paid channel cannot out-saturation the incumbent. The volunteer network can reach places the incumbent's paid saturation does not — specifically, one-on-one conversations that paid impressions cannot replicate at any spend level.

## 6. Competitor Saturation Map

*The brief did not name the incumbent with a proper noun. The analysis remains at the descriptor level: "[3-term incumbent backed by ruling national party]."*

### What [the 3-term incumbent] saturates

- **State TV:** Regional news coverage that presents the incumbent's positions as neutral governance updates, not as campaign content. Structural advantage that the campaign cannot match.
- **Regional billboards:** Visual dominance of physical public space. Every main road into and through the city carries the incumbent's image and messaging. Stability/continuity framing, infrastructure delivery claims.
- **Facebook feed advertising:** Paid placement targeting registered voters in the city, heavy frequency, polished creative. Likely algorithmically optimized for reach among older voter segments.
- **Sponsored newspaper supplements:** Purchased editorial-adjacent content in the regional print press, disguised as journalism. Reinforces the "delivered infrastructure" narrative with photo-heavy "achievements" pages.
- **Emotional register:** Authority, stability, continuity. The visual language is: completed projects, ribbon cuttings, official settings. The message is "we delivered; the alternative is risk."

### The absence that becomes your signal

The incumbent is present everywhere you are supposed to look: TV, billboards, newspaper, paid social. The absence that becomes the candidate's signal is everywhere the incumbent is not looking back: independent Facebook Groups with actual community moderation, WhatsApp threads between neighbors, a Discord where young professionals actually live, the kitchen table, the backroom of the small business, the neighborhood meeting where no journalist from a captured outlet is filing a story.

The candidate's refusal to match the billboard and sponsored-supplement presence is not a weakness — it is the message. "We don't have €1.2 million because we're not taking the money that comes with it" is the positioning, stated or implied by every piece of unpolished, personal, direct content the candidate produces.

### One-sentence positioning line

*"While [the incumbent] talks about what was delivered, we are in your neighborhood, listening to what wasn't."*

This sentence must be the test for every piece of campaign content: does this reinforce the contrast between polished incumbency and present-tense, in-person listening? If yes, it belongs in the campaign. If it tries to match the incumbent's production value or speak to abstract policy, it dilutes the positioning.

## 7. Three Alternative Campaign Shapes

### Shape 1 — Community-First (Recommended Primary Shape)

**Structure:** Start with the 30 existing volunteers and the 800-member Discord. Organize into neighborhood cells before any public-facing content goes live. Each cell is a named local group with a named local coordinator. The candidate shows up to each cell's inaugural meeting — in person, not via video. The cell then operates autonomously: local outreach, local events, local print distribution. The campaign's digital content (candidate Facebook Lives, TikTok videos) feeds material into these cells for peer-to-peer distribution.

**Flagship content (Self/Us/Now):**
- *Self:* The candidate describes the specific moment during city-council work when they understood the incumbent's approach would not change — a concrete meeting, vote, or decision. Real detail, on camera, on a phone, from their kitchen or office.
- *Us:* Every neighborhood in this city has a version of the same problem I heard in [specific neighborhood] last month — [specific issue]. That's not bad luck. That's a pattern.
- *Now:* Join your neighborhood's group this week. We're organizing [specific next meeting date] in [specific neighborhood]. Bring one person who hasn't decided yet.

**Tradeoffs:**
- Slowest to scale publicly, but most durable. Word-of-mouth organized structures outlast any individual campaign moment.
- Requires the candidate to show up in person repeatedly. High time cost on the candidate.
- If volunteer coordinator quality is uneven, some cells will underperform. The campaign needs a volunteer quality process (see first-30-days list).
- Best for: sustained 6-month campaign where the final turnout operation is the decisive factor.

---

### Shape 2 — Earned-Media-First

**Structure:** Lead with one high-credibility newsworthy action in week 1 that creates a story the non-captured local press and regional outlets will cover. The "Open Budget" concept (Concept D above) is one candidate; a policy accountability moment tied to a specific incumbent claim is another. The earned coverage acts as the reach amplifier that the campaign cannot buy — third-party credibility borrowed from the outlet. The community-build and volunteer network build behind the media moment, using the press coverage as social proof.

**Flagship content (Self/Us/Now):**
- *Self:* "I published our entire campaign budget publicly today. Not because it's required — it's not — but because this city deserves to know who funds the people asking for its vote."
- *Us:* "Every voter in this city is making a decision with incomplete information. We're going to change that, one week at a time."
- *Now:* "Read the full budget at [link]. Share it with one person who cares about where the money comes from."

**Tradeoffs:**
- Faster initial reach if the earned-media action lands coverage. One good story in a credible outlet can outperform months of organic posting.
- Dependent on non-captured press outlets actually covering the story. The brief notes local media is "partially captured" — the campaign manager needs to map which outlets are accessible before betting on this shape.
- Risk: if the newsworthy action fails to get coverage, the launch moment is wasted. Earned media requires a genuine news hook, not a press release.
- Best for: campaigns where the candidate has existing relationships with specific independent journalists, and where the newsworthy action is concrete and verifiable.

---

### Shape 3 — Search-Capture / Digital-First for the Young Professional Segment

**Structure:** Concentrate the first 4–6 weeks entirely on the Discord and on TikTok/Instagram content aimed at the 25–40 young professional segment. The thesis: this segment is both under-served by the incumbent's messaging (which is aimed at older, stability-seeking voters) and disproportionately likely to organize, share, and recruit from their own networks. Win this segment first and deeply; use their networks as the amplification layer into the broader electorate.

**Flagship content (Self/Us/Now):**
- *Self:* "I came back to this city [or: I stayed in this city] because I believed it could become what I knew it should be. Three terms later, I'm still waiting. So are you."
- *Us:* "Every person I meet in the Discord who says they almost left — and there are hundreds of you — is describing the same calculation. The city doesn't make it easy to stay. That calculation can change."
- *Now:* "We need 200 new volunteers from this community by [specific date]. If you've been watching this from the Discord, this week is the week to move from watching to doing."

**Tradeoffs:**
- Fastest initial organic engagement within a defined segment.
- Risk of preaching to the converted — the 800 Discord members are already supporters. The test is whether they recruit from outside the Discord.
- TikTok reach for a local mayoral race is unpredictable — one video can go regional, most will reach only the existing network. Do not plan around virality; plan around the consistent recruiting function.
- Best for: campaigns that want to build an energized, visible young-voter core that the local press will cover as a story in itself ("young professionals organizing for the challenger").

## 9. Ad Copy + Boost Rules

**Categorical asymmetry applies. Broad cold-paid is refused for this campaign.**

The €3,500 warm-retargeting allocation in the budget is reserved for the final 6 weeks before election day only. It is not a content promotion channel — it is a turnout reminder tool for people who already know and support the candidate.

### Warm retargeting rules (final 6 weeks only)

**Who to target:** The existing 12k Facebook follower base + anyone who has engaged with candidate content in the past 90 days. Not lookalikes. Not cold audiences. Not interest-targeting against uncommitted voters.

**What to boost:** Only content that has passed the 48-hour organic traction gate. The traction signal for a political campaign: saves, shares, and comment thread depth — not raw likes. A post with 500 likes and 3 shares has no organic traction. A post with 200 likes and 80 shares has real traction.

**Do not boost:** any post that has not demonstrated organic traction in 48 hours. Do not boost polished or produced content. Do not boost anything that looks like a campaign ad. The boosted content should look identical to the organic content — phone video, direct to camera, specific, personal.

**Ad copy direction:** The warmth of the targeting means the "ad" should feel like a direct message from the candidate to someone who already knows them. Not a broadcast. Not a slogan.

- Example direction: "[Candidate first name] here. Election day is [X] weeks away. If you've been following along — thank you. The ask now is simple: vote, and bring one person with you who hasn't decided. Here is what [specific neighborhood initiative/thing that happened] looked like last week." [Attaches real phone video from that week.]

**Frequency cap:** Maximum 3 impressions per person per week on the warm retargeting. After 5–6 impressions, conversion rate drops and reactance rises. Refresh creative every 2 weeks minimum.

**Note on Meta's political ad rules:** As of October 2025, Meta and Google removed political and issue advertising across the EU. Verify the current status of these rules for the specific country before planning any paid activation. If EU political ad bans apply to this city, the entire €3,500 warm retargeting budget re-routes to: additional print production, volunteer event costs, or earned-media activation. The organic campaign plan is unaffected.

## 10. Lift-Test / Measurement Plan

**Budget status:** Zero paid spend for months 1–4 (all organic). Small warm retargeting spend in months 5–6. Template 5 (organic-source attribution) applies for the campaign's primary measurement. Template 4 (micro-lift) applies if warm retargeting activates in month 5.

### Primary measurement: Template 5 — Organic-source attribution

**Hypothesis:** Some organic channels produce higher commitment-conversion rates than others. Identifying the top channel and doubling effort there produces more volunteer signups, more event attendance, and ultimately more committed voter contacts than spreading effort evenly.

**Setup:**
1. UTM-tag every link the campaign publishes. Naming scheme: `utm_source=<channel>` (facebook-personal, facebook-groups, discord, tiktok, whatsapp-referral, print-qr, press-coverage), `utm_medium=organic`, `utm_campaign=mayoral-2026`. Every QR code on print materials gets a unique UTM.
2. Set up a simple conversion tracking page. The primary conversion is: "Volunteer signup form submitted" or "Neighborhood meeting RSVP confirmed." A secondary conversion is: "Candidate Facebook page followed after arriving from a tagged link."
3. Run for 30 days minimum (months 1–2). Pull weekly snapshots.
4. Rank channels by conversion rate per unique visitor — not by absolute volume. 100 Discord-referred signups from 500 sessions (20%) outperforms 300 Facebook-referred signups from 10,000 sessions (3%).

**Decision rule (end of month 1 review):**
- Top channel by conversion rate → double weekly effort next 30 days.
- Any channel below 20% of top channel's conversion rate → drop or reduce to 1 maintenance touchpoint per week, unless it serves a secondary function (print newspaper reaches media-dark zones; drop it from the conversion metric, keep it for the reach function).
- Overall conversion rate below 0.5% across all channels → re-examine the commitment ask. The bottleneck may be the friction of signing up, not the reach. Simplify the ask before adding more channels.

**Caveat stated plainly:** Template 5 is directional, not causal. A volunteer who signed up via the Discord link may have first heard about the campaign from a neighbor, then followed the Facebook page, then clicked the Discord invite. Multi-touch attribution is underspecified. Use the channel rankings to guide effort allocation, not as proof that any single channel caused the outcome.

### Secondary measurement (if warm retargeting activates): Template 4 — Micro-lift

**Design:** Run warm retargeting for 2 weeks in month 5. Add one question to the volunteer signup or event RSVP flow: "How did you hear about this event?" with options including "saw a reminder from the campaign on Facebook." Compare ad-attributed share to organic share.

**Decision threshold:** If fewer than 10% of converters credit the warm retargeting ad: the spend was not material to conversion; shift remaining budget to print and volunteer costs. If 20%+ credit the ad: continue spend, verify frequency cap is holding.

## 11. Anti-Vanity Metric Dashboard

### Track these

| Metric | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Volunteer signups by zone, week-over-week | The volunteer network is the campaign's primary distribution and turnout channel. Zone-level tracking reveals which coordinators are performing and which zones need support. |
| Neighborhood meeting attendance, per meeting | The in-person meeting is the highest-conversion touchpoint in the stack. Attendance rate is a leading indicator of election-day turnout in that zone. |
| Organic content: saves + shares per post (48-hr window) | Saves indicate intent-to-return; shares indicate peer-to-peer distribution. These are the only engagement metrics that predict word-of-mouth spread. |
| UTM-tagged conversion rate per channel (monthly) | Directional signal on which channels are producing committed contacts vs. passive viewers. |
| Press coverage placements in non-captured outlets | Earned media credibility borrowing. Each placement in an independent outlet reaches an audience that the campaign cannot reach directly with its own content. |
| Discord daily active members and Thursday thread reply rate | Leading indicator of whether the community is two-way (real) or one-way (broadcast). If replies drop below 5 per weekly thread, the community is becoming a broadcast channel. |
| Voter contact rate in each zone (tracked by coordinators) | The metric that directly predicts election-day turnout. Zones with 40%+ registered voter contact by week 20 should be the model; zones below 20% need coordinator support or redeployment. |
| New volunteer net additions per week | Growth signal for the ground game. Plateau or decline in new volunteers is a leading warning signal — address the coordinator pipeline before election month. |

### Ignore these

| Metric | Why it is a trap |
|---|---|
| Facebook page impressions | Impressions measure delivery, not persuasion. The incumbent has 100× more impressions and is losing the trust race. |
| Follower count growth | Followers are a passive audience. Committed volunteers and meeting attendees are the active audience. A campaign with 2,000 committed volunteers and 10k followers beats one with 50k followers and 500 volunteers. |
| Raw likes per post | Likes are the cheapest engagement signal and the most easily gamed. Bot accounts, drive-by engagements, and people who click like as a social reflex and forget immediately. |
| Platform-reported ad reach (if warm retargeting runs) | Platform reach numbers are attributed, not incremental. Use the micro-lift question in the signup flow, not platform-reported reach. |
| National or regional poll movement | Polls of this race are too infrequent, too noisy, and too lagged to be actionable. They also measure a different universe than the campaign's zone-by-zone organizing model. |
| Social media engagement rate (aggregate) | Without segmenting by content type, audience segment, and channel, aggregate engagement rate is noise. A high-engagement post in the existing base and a high-engagement post that recruits from outside the base are not the same thing. |
| Total event RSVPs without show-up rate | RSVPs with no show-up confirmation overcount committed supporters. Track attendance, not registration. |

### The standing warning (sector-rider failure mode)

**Fragmented opposition is the single highest-risk failure mode for this campaign.** If other candidates are running against the same incumbent, and if the combined vote share of all challengers exceeds the incumbent's vote share but is split across candidates, the incumbent wins on plurality. The campaign manager should assess the opposition field in week 1 and, if vote-splitting is a real risk, pursue formal or informal agreements (endorsements, non-competition in certain zones, coalition framing) with other challengers. This is a strategic task, not a messaging task — it cannot be fixed by better content.

The second standing warning: **do not mistake content production for organizing.** A well-run TikTok account with 50,000 views per video is not the same as 500 committed volunteers with a plan to contact every voter in their zone. The campaign's bottleneck is organized human contact in the final 30 days, and that bottleneck is not solved by content. Content feeds the network; it is not the network.

## 1. Campaign Ideas

Five concepts across genuinely different archetypes. The brief named LexisNexis; that name is used. No other competitor names are invented.

---

### Concept A — "The $400-Seat Tax"
**Archetype:** Counter-Narrative  
**One-line thesis:** LexisNexis prices family lawyers at enterprise rates designed for BigLaw; naming that gap out loud, with receipts, gives solo and small-firm practitioners a shared grievance and makes you the obvious alternative.  
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (founder LinkedIn + personal X/Twitter) + Tier 2 (earned media in bar association newsletters and legal-tech podcasts)  
**Authenticity hook:** Publish the actual LexisNexis solo-practitioner pricing tier vs. what a small family-law firm actually uses each month — a real usage audit, not a hypothetical. If you don't have a client's permission to publish their numbers, publish your own beta-user's anonymized version.  
**Minimum viable version (2 weeks):** One LinkedIn post from the founder titled "I looked at what family lawyers actually pay LexisNexis for. Here's what the bill looks like vs. what they use." No pitch in the first post — just the receipts and the question. Measure saves and comments from lawyers. If it resonates, you have your campaign.

---

### Concept B — "Office Hours for Family Lawyers"
**Archetype:** Community-Build  
**One-line thesis:** Solo family lawyers are professionally isolated — they have no in-house team to ask, no law-firm library, no one to pressure-test a custody argument with at 10pm. A free, recurring community node with the founder as the host fills that gap and builds the trust moat LexisNexis's enterprise contract cannot match.  
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (community node — Slack or Circle — plus weekly Zoom office hours hosted by founder)  
**Authenticity hook:** The founder is present and personally answers questions from real family lawyers in real time. Not a product demo. A practitioner helping practitioners.  
**Minimum viable version (2 weeks):** DM 15 family lawyers directly — via LinkedIn or bar association member directories where permitted — and invite them to a one-hour "family-law practice problems" Zoom. No product pitch during the session. At the end, mention the tool exists. Gauge how many want to try it.

---

### Concept C — "The Open Playbook"
**Archetype:** Product-Led Growth / Earned-Media  
**One-line thesis:** Publish one complete, practical legal playbook (e.g., "How to build a custody timeline that holds up in court — every form, every cite, every step") as a free, shareable PDF or microsite. The playbook distributes the tool; the tool deepens the playbook.  
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 2 (SEO content + organic LinkedIn + bar association sharing) + Tier 1 (founder voice documenting the build)  
**Authenticity hook:** The playbook is written by or with a real practicing family lawyer — either the founder (if that's the background) or a named co-author who is a current practitioner. Byline from a real attorney changes the trust calculus entirely.  
**Minimum viable version (2 weeks):** Write one 1,500-word guide on a single high-intent topic — custody modification filings, standard visitation schedule wording, or child support calculation worksheets — and post it publicly with a link to the tool. UTM-tag the link. See how many lawyers arrive from the guide.

---

### Concept D — "The Defector Story"
**Archetype:** Founder-Story Arc  
**One-line thesis:** If the founder has a direct experience with the incumbent's pricing, UX, or institutional bias (was a lawyer who used LexisNexis and got frustrated, or built this because a lawyer friend showed them the bill), that lived experience is the campaign — told in the founder's own voice, repeatedly, with specific details.  
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (founder LinkedIn, personal Substack or newsletter, podcast appearances)  
**Authenticity hook:** The specific moment of frustration or decision — not "I saw a market opportunity" but "Here is the exact bill my friend showed me, and here is the exact case type she was researching, and here is what she actually needed from a $400/seat platform." Specificity is the entire hook.  
**Minimum viable version (2 weeks):** Write one 600-word LinkedIn essay titled "Why I stopped paying LexisNexis." Publish under the founder's personal name. Do not make it a product pitch — make it a story. The tool appears in the last two sentences. Measure DMs and comments from lawyers.

---

### Concept E — "Bar Association Insider"
**Archetype:** Coalition Play  
**One-line thesis:** State and county bar associations have family-law sections with newsletters, CLE programs, and member trust. A formal or informal partnership — speaking at a family-law section meeting, sponsoring a CLE, co-authoring a tech-adoption guide — puts the tool inside the trust infrastructure the lawyers already rely on.  
**Primary channel tier:** Tier 1 (direct relationship with bar association program chairs) + Tier 2 (co-branded earned media in association newsletters)  
**Authenticity hook:** A real endorsement or co-presentation with a named family-law section chair or past president. Borrowed credibility from the institution the audience already trusts.  
**Minimum viable version (2 weeks):** Email 5 state bar family-law section program chairs and offer a free 20-minute CLE webinar on "practical legal research for solo family-law practitioners on a tight budget." No product pitch in the title. Success signal: at least one accepts in 14 days.

**Preconditions Check** (scoring against the six factors from `references/hungarian-case-study.md`):

1. **Credible insider / founder-story equivalent.** Likely present — the founder built this tool presumably because they or someone close to them felt the pain of enterprise legal-research pricing. 2. **Accumulated grievance or unmet demand.** Yes — solo and small-firm family lawyers have a documented, recurring, and widely shared frustration with enterprise legal-research pricing that assumes BigLaw budgets. The grievance is real and has been simmering for years. *Score: 1.*
3. **Consolidated challenger field.** Partially — there are other legal-tech tools in the category. However, none has become the named alternative for solo family lawyers specifically. There is a consolidation opportunity if you claim the "family lawyer's tool" positioning before anyone else does. *Score: 0.5 — treat as a campaign sub-goal: claim and hold the category-leader slot for solo family law.*
4. **Felt pain, not abstract pain.** Yes — the billing pain is direct: a solo family lawyer receives the invoice monthly, knows exactly what they paid, and can describe exactly what they used it for. This is not a distributed or abstract grievance. *Score: 1.*
5. **Threshold-rewarding system.** Partially — legal-tech adoption has network effects (bar association word-of-mouth, referrals from colleagues, CLE co-presenter credibility) but is not as threshold-driven as electoral math. *Score: 0.5 — referral and community mechanics can manufacture some of this threshold effect.*
6. **Incumbent overplaying a saturation hand.** Yes — LexisNexis is known for enterprise-polished, high-production marketing that does not speak to the solo practitioner's kitchen-table reality. Their saturation in BigLaw channels leaves the solo practitioner channel substantially uncontested. *Score: 1.*

**Total: 5/6.** Run the organic-first playbook at full scale. The one sub-goal to build during the campaign: claim the consolidated-challenger position for solo family-law practitioners before a competitor does.

## 5. 70/30 Allocation

**Allocation: 95% organic / 5% paid (effectively 100% organic in the first 60 days).**

Rationale: Categorical asymmetry (100×) with a trust bottleneck means broad cold-paid is off the table. There is no budget stated in the brief, and even if there were, the organic-first discipline requires an organic winner before any paid amplification. Allocation in the first 60 days:

- **95% effort → Tier 1 and Tier 2 organic:** founder LinkedIn, SEO content, bar-association outreach, earned media, email to warm list.
- **5% → held in reserve:** if a LinkedIn post or SEO article demonstrates organic traction in 24–48 hours (saves, shares, deep comments — not likes), consider a single targeted boost to a warm retargeting audience. Not before.

**Paid channels explicitly off the table in Phase 1:**
- Cold Meta / Google display: refused. Categorical asymmetry + trust bottleneck = negative ROI.
- Generic Google Ads on competitive keywords: refused. CPC in legal-tech is extremely high; LexisNexis and other incumbents have automated bidding that will outbid you on any broad keyword. Non-brand, long-tail SEO is the correct alternative.
- Any brand-keyword bidding: premature — your brand is unknown; people are not searching your name yet.

## 7. Three Alternative Campaign Shapes

Each shape includes a flagship Self / Us / Now content piece (Marshall Ganz organizing framework).

---

### Shape 1 — Founder-Led Counter-Narrative First

**What it is:** The founder publishes a sustained LinkedIn series over 8 weeks documenting the real cost of enterprise legal research for solo family practitioners — specific receipts, specific use cases, specific frustrations (not invented; sourced from real conversations with users or from public bar-forum discussions). The tool appears as the founder's answer to the problem they are describing.

**Self / Us / Now flagship piece:**
- *Self:* "Here is the specific moment I realized the pricing model for legal research assumed you had a billing rate of $800/hour. I don't. [Most family lawyers] don't."
- *Us:* "Every solo family practitioner I talk to has the same stack: one outdated treatise, a Westlaw subscription they share with someone else, and Google. We're doing $300 research with $20 tools because the good tools are priced for clients none of us have."
- *Now:* "I built something for us. Try it free for 30 days — no annual contract required."

**Tradeoffs:**
- Fastest path to trust with the audience if the founder has a compelling personal story.
- Requires the founder to post consistently on LinkedIn for 8 weeks — if founder is not comfortable with personal professional content, this shape underperforms.
- Highest ceiling if it lands — one viral post from a lawyer sharing "finally someone said this" can drive hundreds of trial signups.
- Lowest floor if the founder does not have a direct lived connection to family law — the counter-narrative feels hollow without a real insider story.

---

### Shape 2 — Open Playbook / Search-Capture First

**What it is:** The first 30 days are spent publishing 3–4 genuinely useful, shareable legal guides on high-intent topics that family lawyers search for (e.g., "child support modification standard in [state]," "parenting plan modification checklist," "how to cite custody case law effectively"). Each guide is free, ungated, and linked to the tool for the research step. SEO compounds over 60–90 days; bar-association members share the guides because they are actually useful.

**Self / Us / Now flagship piece:**
- *Self:* "I spent six months watching family lawyers use workarounds for research tasks the tool should handle — not because the research didn't exist, but because the access cost was prohibitive. So I wrote down every step I'd want documented."
- *Us:* "Every family lawyer knows the 10pm motion panic. You need one case, you know it exists, and you're about to pay $400 for a platform you haven't logged into in three weeks."
- *Now:* "This guide is free. The tool that automates step 4 is free for 30 days. Share it with anyone who has ever Googled 'custody modification standard' at 11pm."

**Tradeoffs:**
- Lower personal-brand risk — works even if the founder is not a natural public communicator.
- Compounding: SEO traffic grows over months even if LinkedIn posting stops.
- Slower to trust-build than Concept 1 — the guides help, but they do not establish a person the audience trusts.
- Requires 3–4 well-researched, genuinely useful pieces (not thin content) or the SEO does not work and the credibility is not built.

---

### Shape 3 — Bar Association Insider First

**What it is:** Spend the first 30 days securing one or two bar association family-law section relationships — a co-presented CLE, a newsletter feature, or a "tools for solo practitioners" webinar. The product earns institutional credibility from the institutions family lawyers already trust. LinkedIn and SEO run in parallel but are secondary.

**Self / Us / Now flagship piece (for the CLE or webinar):**
- *Self:* "I built this tool because I watched a family lawyer bill a client for three hours of legal research that should have taken 45 minutes — not because she was slow, but because the tools she had weren't designed for her case type."
- *Us:* "Solo family-law practice runs on compressed time and compressed budgets. The enterprise tools weren't built for that. They were built for firms that have research librarians."
- *Now:* "If you're in this session, you're already thinking about how to tighten your practice. Here's a 30-day free trial — no credit card, no annual contract. If it doesn't save you time, don't keep it."

**Tradeoffs:**
- Highest credibility signal per hour invested — a bar association endorsement or co-presentation is worth dozens of cold LinkedIn posts.
- Slowest to start — bar association program chairs have their own timelines; getting a CLE slot may take 4–8 weeks.
- Lowest scalability in the first 60 days — if you get one bar association webinar, you get one; it does not compound the way SEO or LinkedIn does.
- Best when combined with Shapes 1 or 2 rather than as the sole strategy.

---

**Recommendation (for Mode C default):** Run Shape 1 (founder counter-narrative) as the primary, with Shape 2 (open playbook / SEO) running in parallel at a lower weekly commitment. Initiate Shape 3 (bar association) as a background track — email 5 program chairs in week 1, accept whoever responds, don't let the waiting block the other two shapes.

## 9. Ad Copy + Boost Rules

**Categorical asymmetry: broad cold-paid is refused.** There is no paid advertising creative produced in this section for cold audiences. LexisNexis spends at a scale (stated 100×) where any cold-paid spend will be invisible relative to their existing saturation.

**What paid could look like — only after organic wins exist:**

If a LinkedIn post demonstrates organic traction in 24–48 hours (saves, shares, deep practitioner comments — not raw likes), a single LinkedIn boosted post to a **warm retargeting audience** (people who have visited the trial signup page or engaged with previous posts) is the one permitted paid action in Phase 1. Rules:

1. **24–48h organic traction gate:** the post must have demonstrated genuine organic signal before any boost is authorized. Saves and shares from lawyers are the signal. Likes from outside the target audience are not.
2. **Audience:** warm retargeting only — website visitors or LinkedIn page engagers from the past 30 days. No cold audiences, no lookalikes in Phase 1.
3. **Frequency cap:** no more than 5–6 impressions to any individual. Beyond 6, you are paying to annoy people who have already decided not to convert. Creative refresh every 2 weeks at most.
4. **Creative direction:** same voice as the organic post — founder's own words, no stock photography, no polished brand language. If it looks like an LexisNexis ad, it is wrong.
5. **Budget ceiling:** $0 until an organic winner is confirmed. If you invest, start at $50–100/week maximum on the warm retarget and run a Template 5 attribution check after 2 weeks to see if it is adding above organic baseline.
6. **No brand-keyword bidding:** you are not large enough for this to matter yet, and the eBay / Blake-Nosko-Tadelis evidence strongly suggests it would cannibalize organic traffic if you were. Do not start.

**If anyone suggests "let's just run some Google Ads" in the first 60 days:** The CPC for legal-research keywords is extremely high (dominated by LexisNexis and Westlaw). You would exhaust any small budget within days producing a CPA that cannot be sustained. Organic demand capture via SEO content on long-tail queries is the correct substitution — it compounds rather than depletes.

## 10. Lift-Test / Measurement Plan

**Zero-budget primary plan: Template 5 — Organic-source attribution.**

**Hypothesis:** LinkedIn organic founder content will outperform all other channels on conversion rate per unique visitor in the first 30 days, because family lawyers are findable on LinkedIn by professional title and respond to peer/founder-voice content from inside their professional network.

**Setup:**
- UTM-tag every link from every channel: `utm_source=linkedin`, `utm_source=newsletter`, `utm_source=seo`, `utm_source=bar-assoc-referral`, `utm_source=earned-media-[publication-slug]`. Medium: `utm_medium=organic`. Campaign: `utm_campaign=legal-tech-family-law-2026`.
- Track one conversion event: trial signup (or demo request form submission). This is the downstream action that matters, not the page view.
- Analytics: whichever tool you already use. If none: Plausible Analytics (privacy-first, no cookie banner required, €9/month) or PostHog free tier. Set up before any content goes live — retroactive attribution is unreliable.

**Measurement window:** 30 days (weeks 1–4 of the action plan above).

**Ranking metric:** conversion rate per unique visitor by UTM source (not absolute conversion volume — a newsletter with 100 clicks and 5 signups outperforms a LinkedIn post with 2,000 impressions and 8 signups).

**Decision rule (Template 5):**
- **Top channel by conversion rate:** double effort on it in month 2.
- **Any channel at <20% of the top channel's conversion rate:** drop it from the week-5+ plan unless it serves a secondary function (SEO backlinks, credibility signal) that the conversion number cannot capture. Document the secondary function explicitly before keeping it.
- **Overall conversion rate across all channels <0.5%:** re-run the MMF gate before month 2. The bottleneck is upstream of channel selection — probably the offer (trial signup page, pricing clarity, onboarding friction) rather than the distribution.

**Caveat — Template 5 is directional, not causal.** Without a randomized holdout, you cannot prove any channel caused its conversions. Someone may have first seen the tool via LinkedIn, then searched Google, then clicked the SEO article, and attributed to the email newsletter. Treat the rankings as a rough guide to effort allocation, not as proof of lift. If the campaign generates any budget in month 2, graduate to Template 1 (geo-holdout) for a real incremental lift measurement.

**Decision threshold:** after 30 days, if the top channel's conversion rate is at least 3× the bottom channel's, the evidence is strong enough to concentrate effort. If all channels are within 1.5× of each other, the limiting factor is probably the offer or the volume — not the channel mix. In that case, optimize the trial signup page before reallocating channel effort.

## 11. Anti-Vanity Metric Dashboard

### Track these (they connect to real outcomes)

| Metric | Why it matters | How to get it |
|---|---|---|
| Trial signups by UTM source | Tells you which channel is actually driving conversions, not just visits | Analytics platform (UTM-tagged) |
| LinkedIn post saves and reshares from lawyers | Saves and shares indicate the content is worth keeping or sharing — not just a scroll-past. Comments from lawyers specifically (not general LinkedIn users) indicate professional resonance. | LinkedIn analytics (saves count visible on personal posts) |
| Reply rate on bar association outreach | Tells you whether the coalition play is working before investing more time | Manual count |
| Earned-media placements confirmed | Real coverage in real publications read by family lawyers | Manual count; track by publication + reach estimate |
| 30-day trial-to-paid conversion rate | The actual retention signal — are lawyers who try it keeping it? | Your product analytics |
| Organic search impressions for target queries (Google Search Console) | Directional signal that SEO content is indexing and being served | Google Search Console |
| Inbound DMs / referrals from lawyers to colleagues | Word-of-mouth signal — the highest-trust channel; any referral that happens organically is worth more than 100 cold impressions | Manual log (ask every new signup "how did you hear about us?") |

### Ignore these (they are noise or actively misleading)

| Metric | Why to ignore it |
|---|---|
| LinkedIn impressions | Impressions include people who scrolled past in 0.5 seconds. They are not evidence of persuasion or interest. |
| LinkedIn follower count | Followers do not equal practitioners who will trial the tool. A practitioner who saves your post and never follows you is more valuable than a follower who scrolls past every week. |
| Website sessions (without conversion segmentation) | Sessions tell you traffic arrived; they tell you nothing about whether it was the right traffic or whether it converted. |
| Platform-reported attributed conversions (if you run any paid) | Platform attribution counts any conversion that happened after an ad view — even if the person would have converted organically anyway. Lewis & Rao (QJE, 2015) showed most digital ad campaigns cannot be statistically distinguished from zero incremental lift. Require a holdout-based lift test before trusting any attributed ROAS number. |
| Raw email open rate (without click or conversion segmentation) | Open rates inflate with Apple MPP; they do not correlate well with intent. Click-to-conversion rate is the metric that matters. |
| Social media likes | Likes are cheap, bot-inflated, and emotionally satisfying. They do not predict trial signups, referrals, or word-of-mouth. Saves and shares are the proxy for actual interest. |
| Press release pickup / PR coverage count | Coverage that reaches family lawyers matters. Coverage that reaches general tech or legal-industry press, with no penetration into solo-practitioner networks, does not produce conversions. Measure coverage by audience fit, not by count. |
